# Hematology (Malignant) Weekly Report — 2026-W19

> Generated: 2026-05-08 | Sources: CrossRef (*Blood*, *Leukemia*, *Haematologica*, *Blood Advances*), OncLive, ASH News, EHA News
> Coverage: 2026-04-27 to 2026-05-08 | Active Phase III trials tracked: 14

## Summary

Five signals dominate this week's literature. **(1)** A new HSPC-derived 24-gene expression score (MPN24) independently predicts overall survival in myelofibrosis across two validation cohorts (HR 9.81, p = 4.0×10⁻¹¹ and HR 6.40, p = 1.8×10⁻⁷), adding prognostic value above DIPSS and MIPSS70 models.[^1] **(2)** ctDNA-based MRD assessment after two cycles of chemotherapy (MRD-2) in the GHSG HD21 Hodgkin lymphoma trial sharply discriminated outcomes: 4-year PFS 36.7% vs 82.2% for MRD-positive vs MRD-negative patients (HR 5.3, 95% CI 2.0–13.8, p = 0.0008).[^2] **(3)** In a phase 1/2 series, ruxolitinib produced rapid clinical and histopathologic improvement in refractory diarrhea following ciltacabtagene autoleucel, a complication associated with 36–50% reported mortality.[^3] **(4)** Long-term follow-up of betibeglogene autotemcel gene therapy for transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia (median 5.9 years) confirmed durable transfusion independence in 90.2% of phase 3 participants, with no insertional oncogenesis.[^4] **(5)** Mosunetuzumab demonstrated an ORR of 40% and CR rate of 20% in relapsed/refractory Richter transformation, with two durable CRs exceeding 20 months and two patients bridged to allogeneic transplant.[^5]

## I. AML — Acute Myeloid Leukemia

A comprehensive review published this week in *Haematologica* summarizes the expanding role of mutational profiling in guiding AML therapy, emphasizing that molecular classification now underpins treatment assignment across newly diagnosed and relapsed settings.[^6] Separately, a multi-omics study of pediatric minimally differentiated AML (AML-M0) in *Leukemia* characterised 23 cases against 1,483 comparator leukemia samples, finding global DNA hypermethylation, frequent loss-of-function RUNX1 alterations (26%) and ETV6 mutations (22%), and activating RAS/FLT3/JAK pathway mutations in 83% of cases. RUNX1 disruption was significantly associated with poor prognosis and conferred reduced in vitro sensitivity to cytarabine and anthracyclines, suggesting that hypomethylating agents and signaling pathway inhibitors may be rational therapeutic strategies for this rare subgroup.[^7]

A real-world single-center retrospective analysis of venetoclax combined with gilteritinib in FLT3-mutated relapsed/refractory AML was published in *Haematologica*, adding to the growing body of evidence supporting this combination in patients lacking access to clinical trials or those with prior FLT3 inhibitor exposure.[^8] A translational study in *Haematologica* demonstrated that fibrinogen upregulates miR-486-5p and suppresses GPR153, activating mTORC2/AKT signaling to accelerate AML progression in xenograft and primary human cell models, identifying a potential therapeutic axis.[^9] The transposable element–PARP axis article in *Blood* highlights the concept of synthetic lethality with PARP inhibition in homologous recombination-proficient leukemias carrying epigenetic gene mutations, positioning this as a candidate combination strategy for clinical investigation.[^10]

## II. ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

A prospective cohort study of 436 adults with Philadelphia chromosome-negative ALL, published in *Blood*, reported that centrally assessed genetics and MRD can accurately allocate patients to allogeneic HSCT versus chemotherapy consolidation. Overall 3-year OS was 64% (95% CI 58–69%); patients who were MRD-negative at end of induction without high genetic risk achieved 81% 3-year OS, compared with 50% for MRD-negative patients with high genetic risk. Early T-cell precursor ALL showed a 3-year OS of 61% (95% CI 37–79%), confirming persistent unmet need in this subgroup.[^11]

In *Leukemia*, a novel CRLF2-targeted antibody-drug conjugate showed potent preclinical anti-leukemic activity against Ph-like ALL cell lines and patient-derived xenografts, representing a potentially tractable therapeutic target in a subset historically associated with poor outcomes.[^12] A review in *Leukemia* addresses the expanding current and emerging role of blinatumomab in pediatric ALL, including MRD-directed treatment de-escalation strategies and the possibility of chemotherapy-free induction in select patients.[^13] A pediatric ALL study in *Haematologica* used single-cell RNA sequencing to show that poverty-exposed children with standard-risk B-ALL exhibit transcriptional signatures of steroid resistance at diagnosis, alongside increased myeloid inflammatory signatures and reduced CD8⁺ T-cell effector capacity, with potential implications for risk-adapted therapy.[^14]

## III. CML — Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

A case report in *Haematologica* describes a myeloid blast phase of CML harboring the novel NUP98::HOXC12 fusion arising from t(11;12)(p15;q13), expanding the catalogue of NUP98 rearrangements encountered at progression.[^15] A *Leukemia* study characterized germline RAS pathway variants in children with CML, finding a subset of pediatric CML cases that may have an inherited predisposition component warranting further genetic counseling guidance.[^16]

_No new signal from randomized trials or approvals this week._

## IV. CLL / SLL — Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

A methodological perspective published in *Leukemia* argues that MRD is not a universally reliable surrogate endpoint for progression-free survival across all novel CLL therapy trials, cautioning against uncritical adoption of MRD as a primary endpoint, particularly in fixed-duration BTK inhibitor regimens where kinetics of MRD clearance differ fundamentally from venetoclax-based regimens.[^17]

The phase 2 GIVeRS trial results, published in *Haematologica*, examined fixed-duration obinutuzumab, ibrutinib, and venetoclax for Richter's transformation to DLBCL. The triplet combination provides an important benchmark for comparison with the mosunetuzumab data described below.[^18] Complementing GIVeRS, mosunetuzumab monotherapy in 20 relapsed/refractory Richter transformation patients achieved an ORR of 40% and CR rate of 20%, with median PFS of 3.4 months and median OS of 10.2 months; two patients experienced CR lasting >20 months and two patients bridged successfully to allogeneic SCT.[^5]

## V. MDS — Myelodysplastic Syndromes

A *Haematologica* analysis addressed patients with MDS carrying concomitant SF3B1 mutation and del(5q), a molecularly complex subset that is distinct from isolated del(5q) and may respond differently to lenalidomide and luspatercept.[^19] A *Leukemia* study examined the in vivo consequences of U2AF1 S34F versus Q157R hotspot mutations, demonstrating that these two mutations induce distinct hematopoietic phenotypes, target gene expression signatures, and splicing patterns in mice and in human samples. Mice with U2af1 S34F showed more severe cytopenias and reduced stem cell repopulating ability compared with Q157R, and the two mutations co-occurred with different somatic co-mutations in patients, arguing against conflation of these variants in clinical risk stratification.[^20]

A clinical perspective in *Blood* by Groarke et al. provides an expert framework for utilizing somatic alterations — including clonal hematopoiesis mutations and chromosomal abnormalities — to distinguish hypoplastic MDS from immune aplastic anemia and inherited bone marrow failure syndromes, and to estimate risk of secondary myeloid neoplasm transformation.[^21]

## VI. MPN — Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (PV / ET / MF)

The most notable MPN publication this week is the MPN24 gene expression score derived from HSPC bulk RNA sequencing of 358 MF patients, with NanoString validation in two independent cohorts of 170 and 100 patients respectively. MPN24-high patients had markedly worse 5-year OS than MPN24-low patients in both cohorts (25.5% vs 86.9%, HR 9.81, 95% CI not reported, p = 4.0×10⁻¹¹; and 23.5% vs 75.9%, HR 6.40, p = 1.8×10⁻⁷). The score retained independent prognostic significance after adjusting for DIPSS, DIPSS-plus, MIPSS70, and MIPSS70+ version 2.0 (p values 1.6×10⁻⁵ to 5.4×10⁻⁴), and was particularly useful for re-stratifying DIPSS-intermediate and MIPSS70-intermediate/high patients — precisely the cohort for whom transplant timing decisions are most challenging.[^1]

A comprehensive review in *Leukemia* outlines the clinical development arc of MDM2 inhibitors in myeloid cancers, noting that while phase III AML trials were negative, recent MF studies have shown clinically meaningful spleen and symptom responses in TP53 wild-type patients. Rational combinations with JAK inhibitors, BCL-2 antagonists, and interferons are the focus of ongoing trials, and emerging MDM2 degraders may improve therapeutic index over first-generation inhibitors.[^22]

A pilot trial reported in *Haematologica* compared thromboprophylaxis with apixaban versus aspirin in JAK2-positive MPN, providing early feasibility data for a prospective randomized approach to VTE prevention in this high-risk population.[^23]

## VII. DLBCL & Aggressive B-cell Lymphoma

A *Blood Advances* analysis of PET scan positivity after chemoimmunotherapy for DLBCL reinforces that 20–40% of Deauville score 4 scans are falsely positive, and that reflex escalation to salvage therapy in these patients may be harmful. The authors highlight that circulating tumor DNA may discriminate residual lymphoma from inflammatory false positives and should inform management decisions.[^24]

Long-term survival data from the TRANSFORM trial of lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel) in second-line large B-cell lymphoma were published in *Blood Advances* this week, confirming durable benefit for patients receiving early CAR T-cell therapy. Additionally, a retrospective analysis of 584 patients treated with CAR T-cell therapy for large B-cell lymphoma across six Australian centers found no significant association between time of infusion (chronobiology) and clinical outcome, suggesting that logistical rather than circadian considerations should drive scheduling decisions.[^25][^26]

The molecular features underlying response and resistance to glofitamab in R/R B-NHL were characterized using longitudinal single-cell RNA sequencing of peripheral blood immune cells. Maintenance of naïve-like, "fresher" cytotoxic T-cell states at early time points was associated with clinical efficacy, while combining glofitamab with 4-1BB costimulation enhanced these populations and produced stronger anti-tumor activity in preclinical models.[^27] Separately, glofitamab was reported to cross the blood-brain barrier and demonstrate activity in both primary and secondary CNS lymphoma in a *Blood Advances* case series.[^28]

## VIII. Indolent Lymphoma (FL, MCL, MZL)

A translational study in *Blood* demonstrates that DC-SIGN expressed on follicular dendritic cells binds oligomannose-type glycans on the surface immunoglobulin of FL cells, inducing adhesion to VCAM-1 via B-cell receptor-proximal kinases and sustaining FL cell survival within lymph node niches. Critically, antibody blockade of DC-SIGN inhibited adhesion and survival of primary FL cells in vitro, proposing a new therapeutic target in the FL microenvironment.[^29]

A population-based cohort analysis from the Netherlands Cancer Registry of 4,751 MCL patients over three decades showed that 5-year relative survival improved from 38% (1989–2000) to 60% (2015–2020, p <0.01), with rituximab maintenance independently associated with reduced mortality in the R-CHOP-treated cohort (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.53–0.90). Patients with progression within 12 months of treatment had a 2-year OS rate of only 25%, underscoring the continued unmet need in early-relapsing MCL.[^30]

## IX. Hodgkin Lymphoma & T-cell Lymphoma

Two high-impact Hodgkin lymphoma publications appeared simultaneously in *Blood* this week. The phase 2 Intergroup E4412 study randomized 147 patients (≥12 years) with relapsed/refractory classic HL to brentuximab vedotin plus nivolumab (BV/Nivo) or BV plus ipilimumab plus nivolumab (BV/Ipi/Nivo). The primary endpoint of superior CR rate for the triplet was not met (64.7% BV/Nivo vs 70.3% BV/Ipi/Nivo; 1-sided p = 0.29), and PFS did not significantly differ between arms (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.39–1.57; 1-sided p = 0.24). However, among the 66 progression-free patients who did not undergo SCT, 36-month PFS was 73.0% with BV/Ipi/Nivo versus 45.8% with BV/Nivo (HR 0.45, 95% CI 0.19–1.08; 1-sided p = 0.03), generating a hypothesis-generating signal for patients who wish to defer or avoid autologous SCT.[^31]

The MRD-2 analysis from the GHSG HD21 trial, using the LymphoVista validated ctDNA sequencing assay, showed that MRD positivity after two cycles discriminated 4-year PFS outcomes with high resolution (36.7% vs 82.2%; HR 5.3, 95% CI 2.0–13.8; p = 0.0008). After inverse probability weighting, MRD-positive and MRD-negative patients had estimated 4-year PFS rates of 72.2% versus 95.3%. The combination of ctDNA MRD-2 with interim PET-2 identified patients at very low and very high risk of relapse, suggesting complementary rather than competing roles for these biomarkers.[^2]

A brief communication in *Blood* describes a case of classic Hodgkin lymphoma harboring an IGH::CCND1 rearrangement with clonal evidence linking it to preceding mantle cell lymphoma, raising important diagnostic and biological questions about lineage plasticity.[^32]

An *Haematologica* study reported that SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex alterations define a subgroup of adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma with significantly inferior outcomes, providing a molecular basis for further stratification of this aggressive malignancy.[^33]

The CTCL-PRO-20, a novel 20-item patient-reported outcome instrument for cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, was validated in a cross-sectional study of 80 CTCL patients, demonstrating high internal-consistency reliability (Cronbach's α 0.93) and strong convergent validity with disease-specific QoL measures.[^34]

## XI. Multiple Myeloma

The landscape of dual-antigen targeting in myeloma received a thorough conceptual review in *Blood*, addressing how simultaneous targeting of BCMA and GPRC5D can overcome tumor heterogeneity and antigen escape — the dominant mechanisms of resistance to single-antigen T-cell immunotherapies. The authors discuss emerging trispecific antibodies (ramantamig BCMA×GPRC5D; ISB 2001 BCMA×CD38), the combination of teclistamab plus talquetamab (which appears to have enhanced anti-myeloma activity versus single-agent bispecifics in comparable patient populations), and the current lack of evidence showing superiority of dual-targeting CAR-T products over ciltacabtagene autoleucel monotherapy.[^35]

A phase 1/2 study published in *Blood Advances* evaluated belantamab mafodotin (belamaf) at 1.9 mg/kg every 8 weeks in combination with carfilzomib, lenalidomide, and dexamethasone (KRd-b) in 19 patients with RRMM. The ORR was 89.5%, with ≥VGPR in 78.9% of patients and MRD negativity in all complete responders. At median follow-up of 19.3 months, 24-month PFS and OS rates were 74.3% and 85.1%, respectively. Grade ≥3 keratopathy occurred in 31.6% of patients — comparable to historical rates despite extended 8-week dosing intervals — suggesting that less-frequent belamaf administration within an active backbone may maintain efficacy while managing ocular toxicity.[^36]

The plasma cell leukemia "How I Treat" review in *Blood* synthesizes current management evidence for this ultra-high-risk entity, including the emerging roles of BCMA- and GPRC5D-directed CAR-T therapy and bispecific antibodies, while emphasizing the near-complete exclusion of PCL patients from pivotal myeloma trials and the urgent need for dedicated prospective studies.[^37] A *Leukemia* perspective by Landgren argues that the recent FDA approval of daratumumab for high-risk smoldering myeloma marks a paradigm shift, with anticipation that further molecular refinement will reclassify portions of current SMM as either early myeloma (warranting treatment) or benign monoclonal gammopathy (warranting only surveillance).[^38]

## XII. Transplant & Cellular Therapy (CAR-T / HSCT / GVHD)

The ruxolitinib series for ciltacabtagene autoleucel-associated refractory diarrhea is the most clinically actionable CAR-T toxicity report of the week. Among five patients presenting with severe post-BCMA CAR-T diarrhea (a syndrome carrying 36–50% reported mortality), three received ruxolitinib and all demonstrated rapid clinical improvement; matched pre- and post-treatment biopsies in two of these patients showed histopathologic response, with one case representing CAR T-cell-associated indolent T-cell lymphoproliferative disease of the GI tract.[^3] An accompanying editorial in *Blood* provides a supporting argument for prioritizing ruxolitinib over empirical escalation of steroids or other immunosuppression in this setting.[^39]

Anti-CD117 (KIT) CAR T-cells were shown in *Leukemia* to efficiently lyse neoplastic mast cell lines, SM patient-derived iPSC-derived mast cells, and primary bone marrow cells from advanced systemic mastocytosis patients in vitro, with inhibition of mast cell expansion in an immunocompromised xenograft model. This preclinical study positions anti-CD117 CAR-T therapy as a candidate approach for a disease currently dependent on kinase inhibitors (avapritinib, midostaurin) for most patients.[^40]

A phase 1/2 study of donor-derived regulatory T-cell (Treg) infusions for steroid-refractory/dependent chronic GVHD, extended to include 33 patients total, showed that global responses were observed in 71% of patients, with 52% achieving ≥2-point improvement in cGVHD severity scale. Reductions in corticosteroids, ruxolitinib, and mycophenolate were achieved in 58%, 83%, and 33% of patients, respectively, and calcineurin inhibitors were discontinued in 75%. Infused Treg clonotypes were detectable up to 12 months post-infusion.[^41]

Long-term outcomes among older (≥60 years) BMT survivors from the BMTSS reported a 72% prevalence of severe/life-threatening chronic health conditions versus 51% in matched sibling controls (OR 2.9, 95% CI 2.2–3.7), with overall SMR of 5.3 (95% CI 5.0–5.6) and a particularly high infection-related mortality SMR of 35.2 (95% CI 30.3–40.6).[^42]

A murine study in *Blood* characterized the respective roles of XCR1⁺ and CD11b⁺ conventional dendritic cell subsets in gut GVHD, showing that XCR1⁺ cDC deletion attenuated lethal GVHD by suppressing donor Th1 expansion, while ALDH1A1 inhibition (via WIN18446) specifically blocked CD11b⁺ cDC-mediated Th17 differentiation in the gut. Selective targeting of cDC subsets thus emerges as a mechanistically refined strategy for GVHD prevention.[^43]

## XIV. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings this week carry direct relevance for clinical practice in Taiwan. The MPN24 gene expression score requires NanoString infrastructure, which is available at academic centers including NTUH, CGMH, and CMUH; validation in Asian MF patient cohorts will be important given the higher prevalence of CALR type 2 and MPL mutations in East Asian populations compared with Western cohorts. The ruxolitinib data for CAR-T enterocolitis are immediately actionable for centers running ciltacabtagene autoleucel programs; NHI coverage for cilta-cel remains under review, but early-access programs at NTUH and CGMH are treating patients.

The GHSG HD21 ctDNA MRD-2 data reinforce the importance of liquid biopsy infrastructure for HL risk-adapted treatment — EHA and ASH guidelines have not yet formalized MRD-directed de-escalation in HL, but academic centers in Taiwan should consider establishing validated ctDNA platforms. The betibeglogene autotemcel long-term follow-up is highly relevant given Taiwan's β-thalassemia carrier rate of approximately 1–2% and the significant transfusion-dependent population managed across NTUH, CGMH, and Taipei VGH; beti-cel received FDA approval in 2022 but NHI coverage discussions in Taiwan are ongoing.

The MCL rituximab maintenance survival data from the Netherlands registry (HR 0.69 for OS reduction) provide real-world validation supporting the current NCCN/EHA-recommended maintenance strategy, which is reimbursed under NHI for eligible MCL patients. Clinicians at NCKUH managing MCL should note that patients in PR after R-CHOP induction may derive the greatest OS benefit from rituximab maintenance.

## XV. Key Takeaways

The MPN24 HSPC-derived transcriptomic score provides the most significant MF risk stratification advance published in recent months, with large effect sizes in two independent cohorts and additive value over all current scoring systems; it may inform transplant referral timing decisions once NanoString-based clinical assays are standardized. In Hodgkin lymphoma, the HD21 ctDNA MRD-2 data establish early liquid biopsy as a powerful prognostic tool that may eventually guide BrECADD dose adaptation, while the E4412 final analysis clarifies that BV/Ipi/Nivo does not improve CR rate over BV/Nivo for unselected relapsed/refractory HL but may sustain durable disease control in the transplant-deferral setting. In myeloma, the belantamab mafodotin every-8-week schedule embedded in KRd is an important step toward rendering this ADC more tolerable within intensive triplet/quadruplet backbones, and the dual-targeting review in *Blood* sets a conceptual roadmap for next-generation trial design. For CAR-T programs, ruxolitinib should be prospectively incorporated into management algorithms for post-BCMA-CAR-T diarrhea given the otherwise high mortality of this syndrome.
