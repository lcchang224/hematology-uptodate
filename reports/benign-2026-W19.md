## I. Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

Two clinically relevant papers on ITP appeared this week, spanning paediatric chronicity prediction and immunogenetics.

Xue and colleagues published a prospective observational cohort study using sex-stratified proteomic and metabolomic profiling to identify predictors of chronic ITP in 67 children at diagnosis, followed for at least one year (Xue Y et al., *British Journal of Haematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70521). Integrative multi-omics were analysed with a Pearson correlation threshold of |r| > 0.8, p < 0.05. In male patients, intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1) and biopterin were identified as predictive biomarkers for chronic progression, while in females, ACTA-2 (actin, alpha 2, smooth muscle, aorta) and N6-acetyl-L-lysine were the discriminating factors. Cross-sex application of these biomarkers yielded limited predictive value, underscoring the biological significance of sex dimorphism in ITP pathogenesis. Receiver operating characteristic curves, calibration curves, and clinical decision curve analysis demonstrated good model performance for both sex-specific models. While sample sizes within each subgroup remain small, this framework lays the groundwork for precision prognostic triage at diagnosis, reducing the current reliance on empiric watchful waiting.

In parallel, González de Pablo et al. conducted a retrospective observational study of 135 paediatric primary ITP patients versus 929 healthy controls, performing HLA typing to identify susceptibility and prognostic alleles (González de Pablo J et al., *British Journal of Haematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70483). The median age of ITP patients was 5.71 years (IQR 2.71–10.21). HLA-DRB1*07 and HLA-DRB1*13 were more prevalent in controls, suggesting a potentially protective role (p < 0.05). Conversely, HLA-A*68, HLA-B*40, and HLA-DRB1*14 were associated with increased ITP risk (p < 0.05). Regarding disease course, HLA-A*26 correlated with lower recovery rates, whereas HLA-C*03 was associated with higher recovery likelihood (p < 0.05). HLA-A*02, HLA-C*04, and the HLA-A02 supertype were significantly associated with chronicity (p < 0.05). Although larger validation cohorts are needed, these findings suggest HLA typing could complement clinical and laboratory parameters to identify children at risk for persistent disease.

A focused review by Vijenthira, Frederiksen, and Sholzberg (*BJH*, DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70498) addresses the risk of diagnostic misclassification of ITP in the setting of occult haematological malignancy, the interplay between autoimmunity and clonal haematopoiesis, and downstream oncological complications. The authors argue for a more systematic evaluation protocol when thrombocytopenia is isolated, particularly in older patients, cautioning against reflexive ITP labelling without adequate clonal exclusion work-up.

## III. Aplastic Anemia

A significant conceptual contribution appeared in *Haematologica* from Bierings and Thus, commenting on the resurgent role of horse anti-thymocyte globulin (hATG) in immune-mediated bone marrow failure among very young children (Bierings M, Thus K; *Haematologica*, 2026; DOI: 10.3324/haematol.2026.301004). Although the letter itself does not present new trial data, it references the evolving evidence that hATG — long considered less potent than rabbit ATG in adults — may retain distinct immunological and tolerability advantages in the paediatric population under age three. This commentary arises in the context of ongoing debate regarding optimal immunosuppressive sequencing in very young children who lack suitable transplant donors, and is particularly relevant to NCKUH practice given the frequency of severe aplastic anemia in the Taiwanese paediatric population.

## VII. Thalassemia (alpha & beta; Taiwan-relevant)

Two papers with direct relevance to haemolytic severity assessment in thalassemia were published this week.

Kiger and colleagues introduced a novel plasma heme spectral assay capable of simultaneously quantifying plasma haemoglobin, methaemoglobin, heme, and hemopexin in patients with beta-thalassaemia (β-thal) and sickle cell disease (SCD), providing mechanistic differentiation between intravascular and extravascular haemolysis (Kiger L et al., *American Journal of Hematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70314). In the cohort studied, plasma haemoglobin was significantly higher in SCD than β-thal (6.20 μM vs. 2.52 μM, p < 0.001), consistent with predominantly intravascular haemolysis in SCD. In contrast, β-thal patients exhibited markedly higher plasma heme values (11.00 μM vs. 1.51 μM; p < 0.0001), reflecting a mixed origin of haemolysis from dyserythropoiesis and peripheral destruction. Plasma hemopexin was negatively correlated with plasma heme across both conditions. Excess heme exceeded hemopexin scavenging capacity in 72% of β-thal patients and 36% of SCD patients. Critically, in β-thal, plasma heme was significantly higher in transfusion-dependent compared to non-transfusion-dependent patients, indicating that free heme burden tracks clinical disease severity and may serve as a biomarker guiding transfusion threshold decisions. In SCD, elevated excess heme was associated with a significant increase in mortality risk compared to LDH or reticulocyte percentage as comparator biomarkers. This novel assay may refine haemolysis monitoring beyond traditional indirect surrogates, with potential application in Taiwanese thalassaemia management programmes where non-transfusion-dependent thalassaemia represents a substantial proportion of the follow-up cohort.

## IX. Red Cell Disorders (G6PD, PK deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis; ETV6-related thrombocytopenia)

Tesakov and colleagues published a mechanistic study of ETV6-related thrombocytopenia (ETV6-RT), an inherited platelet disorder caused by germline ETV6 variants (Tesakov I et al., *British Journal of Haematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70495). The investigators studied 12 patients from six families using functional platelet assays, electron microscopy, quantitative proteomics, and cytoskeletal imaging. Clinically, patients exhibited mild-to-moderate thrombocytopenia with variable paediatric bleeding (median ISTH-BAT score 3, range 1–9) but consistently mild bleeding in adulthood (median 0, range 0–1). Ex vivo thrombus formation was reduced independently of platelet count. Electron microscopy revealed spheroid morphology, reduced Dmax/Dmin ratios, and diminished alpha-granule pools. Proteomics demonstrated reduced alpha-granule and lysosomal proteins alongside imbalanced actin remodelling regulators and β-tubulin. Phalloidin staining confirmed impaired actin cytoskeletal remodelling with reduced lamellipodia formation, and immunofluorescence showed abnormal β1-tubulin localisation with disrupted marginal bands and reduced LAMP1 expression. The authors additionally found impaired granulocyte recruitment and migration within thrombi, suggesting a broader thromboinflammatory defect beyond platelet dysfunction per se. These findings provide mechanistic insight into why ETV6-RT produces bleeding that is disproportionate to platelet count in some patients, and support cytoskeletal integrity as a functional target for evaluation in other inherited platelet disorders.

A global systematic review and meta-analysis on the contemporary burden of sickle cell anaemia in children by Pimenta et al. is also relevant here (*BJH*, DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70501). Among 57 included studies encompassing over 3.6 million individuals and 56,593 recorded deaths, the global under-20 population-based prevalence of SCD was 0.009 (95% CI 0.005–0.017), and the under-20 cause-specific mortality proportion was 0.029 (95% CI 0.001–0.621). The under-5 and 5–19 age group mortality proportions were 0.021 and 0.017, respectively. The wide confidence intervals reflect extreme heterogeneity and substantial gaps in geographic coverage, particularly from sub-Saharan Africa. This synthesis underscores the ongoing inadequacy of global SCD surveillance infrastructure and the urgent need for newborn screening expansion — a consideration also applicable to Taiwan's immigrant and overseas-born populations.

## X. Iron Deficiency & Nutritional Anemias

Cool and colleagues published a real-world analysis examining underdosing of intravenous iron in hospitalised patients with iron deficiency anaemia (Cool J et al., *American Journal of Hematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70329). The abstract data were not fully available via the current feed, but this institutional audit addressing the gap between guideline-recommended total iron replacement doses and actual clinical practice is highly relevant. ## XI. Thrombosis & Coagulation (VTE, APS, inherited thrombophilia; HIT)

The most clinically urgent paper this week in the thrombosis domain concerns heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) diagnostics. Kanack and colleagues report a case series and functional testing screen identifying pathogenic PF4-dependent platelet-activating antibodies in patients with negative PF4/polyvinylsulfonate (PF4/PVS) ELISA results (Kanack A et al., *American Journal of Hematology*, 2026; DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70333). Three index patients with clinical presentations consistent with HIT tested negative on the PF4/PVS ELISA — a commonly used antigenic target for HIT screening — yet all three demonstrated PF4-dependent platelet activation in functional assays, inhibitable by blockade of platelet FcγRIIa receptors and by high-concentration heparin, confirming pathogenic HIT antibody biology. Functional testing-based screening of 500 ELISA-negative patients identified three additional patients with similarly pathogenic antibody profiles. Critically, five of the six ELISA-negative HIT patients were re-exposed to heparin following the negative ELISA result: all five experienced platelet count decline after re-exposure, and one patient developed a new thrombosis. These findings directly challenge current guidelines that endorse ELISA negativity as sufficient to exclude HIT and safely resume heparin. The authors argue that a negative PF4/PVS ELISA should not be used as a standalone rule-out test when clinical probability (e.g., 4T score) remains intermediate or high. For centres such as NCKUH, where PF4/polyanion ELISA is the standard first-line HIT screen, this report mandates review of reflex functional assay protocols in patients with discordant pre-test probability and ELISA results.

In the domain of cancer-associated VTE, Tan and colleagues provide a retrospective real-world comparison of extended reduced-dose apixaban versus full-dose apixaban for cancer-associated venous thromboembolism (*BJH*, DOI: 10.1111/bjh.70513). Full quantitative outcomes data were not extractable from the current abstract text, but the study addresses a clinically pressing question regarding whether dose reduction during the extended treatment phase (beyond 6 months) preserves efficacy while reducing bleeding, paralleling the AMPLIFY-EXT and CARAVAGGIO datasets in high-risk cancer populations.

A commentary by Merriman in *Research and Practice in Thrombosis and Haemostasis* (DOI: 10.1016/j.rpth.2026.106622) addresses VTE risk following surgery for pancreatic neuroendocrine neoplasms, a population with poorly characterised individual thrombotic risk that is underrepresented in current thromboprophylaxis scoring systems.

## XIII. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings in this week's report carry direct relevance to clinical practice at National Cheng Kung University Hospital (NCKUH) and affiliated centres across Taiwan.

**Thalassemia** remains highly prevalent in Taiwan, with alpha-thalassemia carrier rates of approximately 4–5% and beta-thalassemia carrier rates of 1–2% in the general population. The novel plasma heme spectral assay reported by Kiger et al. (DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70314) has particular applicability to the substantial cohort of non-transfusion-dependent thalassemia (NTDT) patients followed at NCKUH, where decisions around initiating or escalating transfusion therapy often rely on indirect haemolysis biomarkers (LDH, bilirubin, haptoglobin) that do not capture free heme burden. Adoption of this assay in clinical research protocols could improve monitoring of iron-mediated organ toxicity in thalassemic patients.

**G6PD deficiency** affects approximately 3% of males in Taiwan. While no new G6PD-specific trial data were published this week, the mechanistic work on ETV6-related thrombocytopenia (Tesakov et al.) is a reminder that inherited red cell and platelet disorders overlap functionally in Taiwan's hereditary anaemia screening programmes; ETV6-RT may be underdiagnosed given its mild adult phenotype.

**ITP** management in Taiwan is covered by NHI for first-line corticosteroids, IVIG, and second-line thrombopoietin receptor agonists (eltrombopag, romiplostim). The new data on HLA-associated ITP susceptibility and chronicity (González de Pablo et al.) are particularly relevant given the distinct HLA allele frequencies in Han Chinese and Taiwanese Aboriginal populations compared to European cohorts. NCKUH's paediatric haematology division may consider incorporating HLA typing into prospective ITP registries to validate these susceptibility alleles in a local cohort.

**HIT diagnostics**: PF4/polyanion ELISA is standard at most Taiwanese tertiary centres for HIT exclusion. The Kanack et al. case series (DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70333) is a direct call to action for haematologists at NCKUH and NTUH to audit reflex functional testing protocols for patients with discordant clinical probability and ELISA results. Heparin is widely used in cardiac surgery, extracorporeal membrane oxygenation, and renal replacement therapy settings, making ELISA-negative true HIT a high-stakes diagnostic pitfall.

**Eculizumab and iptacopan** for PNH are currently available in Taiwan under NHI special permit and rare disease drug provisions, respectively. Emicizumab for haemophilia A with inhibitors has NHI coverage at designated haemophilia treatment centres, including NCKUH.

## XIV. Key Takeaways

The most clinically urgent finding this week is the identification of pathogenic HIT antibodies in patients with negative PF4/polyanion ELISA results (Kanack et al., *AJH*, DOI: 10.1002/ajh.70333). Five of six such patients experienced thrombocytopenia recurrence or new thrombosis upon heparin re-exposure, directly challenging the guideline-embedded practice of using ELISA negativity as a rule-out for HIT. All centres using PF4/polyanion ELISA as a standalone HIT exclusion test should review their functional testing reflex criteria.

In paediatric ITP, two complementary papers this week advance the case for precision prognostics. The sex-stratified omics model (Xue et al.) and HLA susceptibility/chronicity mapping (González de Pablo et al.) together suggest that early identification of children at risk for chronic ITP is feasible, potentially sparing unnecessary treatment escalation in those likely to achieve spontaneous remission while enabling earlier intervention in high-risk individuals.

The plasma heme spectral assay validated by Kiger et al. in SCD and beta-thalassemia offers a biologically direct measure of haemolytic severity that outperforms conventional surrogates in mortality prediction (SCD) and transfusion threshold calibration (beta-thalassemia). Prospective validation in larger cohorts, including Asian thalassaemia populations, is warranted.

The EPO-thromboembolism association in SCD (Zhang et al., OR = 1.9, p = 0.0029) raises the question of whether EPO monitoring should be incorporated into SCD vascular risk stratification, particularly in patients with low haemoglobin F levels or the BCL11A risk genotype at the rs322139 locus.

Finally, the mechanistic characterisation of ETV6-related thrombocytopenia (Tesakov et al.) provides a compelling model linking cytoskeletal integrity and granule biogenesis to functional haemostasis, with implications for risk counselling in carriers of germline ETV6 variants and for the differential diagnosis of unexplained mild thrombocytopenia with disproportionate bleeding in children and young adults.
