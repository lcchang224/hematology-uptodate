## Summary

The five most clinically actionable signals this week are as follows. First, a UK multicenter observational study of 500 patients with thrombotic antiphospholipid syndrome (APS) documented a 46% recurrence rate over the past decade, with lupus anticoagulant presence as the strongest predictor of recurrence (p < 0.01), and a 10-year major bleeding rate of 6.7%[^1]. Second, a prospective sex-stratified proteomic/metabolomic study identified ICAM-1 and biopterin (males) and ACTA-2 and N6-acetyl-L-lysine (females) as candidate biomarkers predicting chronicity in paediatric ITP, with cross-sex application showing limited predictive value[^2]. Third, a case series in AJH describes six patients with ELISA-negative heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) confirmed by functional PF4-dependent platelet activation assays, with heparin re-exposure precipitating thrombocytopenia in five and a new thrombus in one — challenging the exclusionary value of PF4/polyanion ELISA[^3]. Fourth, a preclinical Blood Advances study demonstrates that halofuginone suppresses hepcidin via a heparan sulfate proteoglycan–dependent mechanism, reducing expression in iron-overload and acute inflammation mouse models, providing a candidate oral hepcidin antagonist for iron-restrictive anemias[^4]. Fifth, real-world registry data from RETRO and PROSPECT confirmed that voxelotor increased hemoglobin by 0.6–0.8 g/dL and reduced hemolysis markers in sickle cell disease (SCD) patients in US clinical practice, with no evidence of increased acute pain crisis frequency — providing the largest post-market safety dataset before the drug's withdrawal from global markets in September 2024[^5].

## I. Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

Two independent reports this week significantly advance the molecular understanding of ITP. In a non-interventional prospective observational cohort of 67 children with newly diagnosed ITP and 40 healthy controls, Xue et al. employed integrated plasma proteomic and metabolomic profiling to develop sex-stratified prediction models for chronic ITP[^2]. After a minimum of 12 months of follow-up, analyses using Pearson's correlation (threshold |r| > 0.8, p < 0.05) identified intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1) and the pteridine metabolite biopterin as predictive factors in males, whereas actin alpha-2 smooth muscle aorta (ACTA-2) and N6-acetyl-L-lysine emerged as relevant in females. Receiver operating characteristic, calibration curve, and clinical decision curve analyses demonstrated good predictive performance. Critically, cross-sex application of these biomarkers showed limited predictive value, reinforcing that ITP chronicity may be mechanistically sex-dimorphic. Validation in larger, ethnically diverse cohorts is necessary before clinical implementation.

In parallel, a retrospective Spanish cohort study evaluated the prognostic significance of HLA alleles in 135 paediatric ITP patients compared with 929 healthy controls (median patient age 5.71 years, IQR 2.71–10.21)[^6]. HLA-DRB1*07 and HLA-DRB1*13 were more prevalent in healthy controls, suggesting a protective role (p < 0.05), while HLA-A*68, HLA-B*40, and HLA-DRB1*14 were associated with increased ITP susceptibility (p < 0.05). Regarding prognosis, HLA-A*26 correlated with lower recovery rates and HLA-A*02, HLA-C*04, and the HLA-A02 supertype were significantly associated with higher probability of chronicity (p < 0.05). HLA-C*03 was associated with higher recovery likelihood (p < 0.05). These findings are hypothesis-generating and suggest HLA typing may eventually contribute to individualized prognostic stratification at ITP diagnosis, although the study's retrospective design and single-centre recruitment require cautious interpretation.

A report from *Haematologica* this week raises an important safety signal related to thrombopoietin receptor agonist (TPO-RA) use in paediatric ITP[^7]. Ma et al. document that overuse of TPO-RAs driven by suboptimal response is associated with myelofibrosis in affected children, underscoring the need for careful benefit–risk assessment before escalating or prolonging TPO-RA therapy in young patients, particularly in the absence of bleeding symptoms. Quantitative data from the abstract were not available at time of report compilation; the full manuscript should be consulted when published.

## III. Aplastic Anemia

An editorial commentary published in *Haematologica* revisits the role of horse anti-thymocyte globulin (hATG) in immune-mediated marrow failure in very young children[^9]. Bierings and Thus comment on recent data suggesting that hATG remains a viable and potentially preferred immunosuppressive backbone in this population — a clinically important consideration given the ongoing limited availability of matched sibling donors and questions surrounding horse versus rabbit ATG response rates in young children. No new phase III trial data were published this week.

An earlier EHA news abstract (Dr. Liri Seraj) from the web cache describes a retrospective 10-year epidemiological analysis of acquired aplastic anemia[^10], though no quantitative results were available in the summary captured. This abstract is noted for surveillance purposes.

## V. Hemophilia A & B (including gene therapy)

Two important preclinical and methodological papers appeared this week. Chou et al. (Blood Advances) developed a novel human hemophilia A mouse model (HemoAhfIX/hfX) via adeno-associated virus delivery of human F9 and F10 genes, achieving human FIX levels of 158.0 ± 57.9 IU/dL and FX levels of 372.8 ± 170.7 IU/dL at 12 weeks[^15]. Emicizumab treatment in this model significantly shortened activated partial thromboplastin time and partially restored thrombin generation capacity. Using a 2-factor factorial design across 55 mice, inhibitor titers varied significantly across four treatment groups (P = 0.0098), with notably lower titers in emicizumab-treated animals (P = 0.0006). Emicizumab treatment also preserved joint architecture with reduced synovitis. This model constitutes a valuable preclinical platform for evaluating inhibitor risk during emicizumab prophylaxis when FVIII is co-administered in high-risk scenarios — a question of direct clinical relevance to centers managing inhibitor patients transitioning to emicizumab.

From the thrombosis and haemostasis literature, the ISTH news feed indexed a resource on the 2024 ISTH Clinical Practice Guideline for treatment of congenital hemophilia A and B[^16]. The full guideline was published in mid-2024 and should be consulted for updated evidence-based treatment recommendations.

A *Research and Practice in Thrombosis and Haemostasis* commentary highlights that menstrual outcomes are frequently overlooked in von Willebrand disease (VWD) clinical trials[^17], with O'Donnell et al. advocating for systematic inclusion of patient-reported menstrual bleeding outcomes as endpoints in VWD research — a gap of particular relevance to the management of female patients with all subtypes of VWD. No quantitative data were included in the published abstract.

## VII. Thalassemia (alpha & beta; Taiwan-relevant)

An ASH CONSA report (Blood Advances) is worth noting in this section for its haemoglobin variant detection data from sub-Saharan Africa[^19]. Among 175,000 newborn dried blood spot samples collected through November 2025 across seven African countries, HbSβ+ thalassemia accounted for 7.5% of positive SCD screening results, and hemoglobin C trait had an overall incidence of 1.59%. While primarily an SCD surveillance report, the concurrent detection of β-thalassemia variants reinforces the importance of newborn screening programs capable of identifying compound heterozygous hemoglobinopathies — particularly relevant for Taiwan, where β-thalassemia trait prevalence is approximately 1–2% and interaction with other hemoglobin variants can occur in ethnically diverse populations.

## IX. Red Cell Disorders (G6PD, PK deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis)

Pimenta et al. (BJH) published the first global systematic review and meta-analysis of SCD burden in individuals aged under 5 and under 20, encompassing data from over 3.6 million individuals and 56,593 recorded deaths across 57 studies[^20]. The global under-20 population-based prevalence of SCD was 0.009 (95% CI: 0.005–0.017), and the global under-5 prevalence was 0.008 (95% CI: 0.004–0.016). The under-20 cause-specific mortality proportion was 0.029 (95% CI: 0.001–0.621), with under-5 and 5–19 year mortality proportions estimated at 0.021 and 0.017, respectively. The extreme heterogeneity and wide uncertainty intervals reflect the fragmented nature of global SCD surveillance — a finding that reinforces the urgency of programs such as CONSA for improved screening infrastructure.

A translational study from Zhang et al. (BJH) found that higher serum erythropoietin (EPO) concentration was associated with increased thromboembolic risk in SCD patients from two independent cohorts (UIC and Walk-PHaSST), with a combined odds ratio of 1.9 (p = 0.0029, N = 557)[^21]. The G allele of rs322139 (an expression quantitative trait locus for the EPO receptor gene EPOR) was associated with higher EPOR expression (β = 0.055, p = 2.0 × 10⁻⁵) and lower EPO concentration in both Walk-PHaSST (β = −0.23, p = 6.4 × 10⁻⁵) and UIC (β = −0.18, p = 0.017) cohorts, with a trend toward decreased thromboembolism (combined OR = 0.64, p = 0.054, N = 665). These data identify a novel EPO/EPOR axis that may modulate thrombotic risk in SCD, providing a potential pharmacogenomic rationale for refining anticoagulation strategies in high-EPO SCD patients.

Regarding platelet disorders, Tesakov et al. (BJH) characterized the platelet dysfunction underlying ETV6-related thrombocytopenia (ETV6-RT) in 12 patients from six families using functional assays, electron microscopy, quantitative proteomics, and cytoskeletal imaging[^22]. Most patients exhibited mild-to-moderate thrombocytopenia with variable bleeding symptoms (median ISTH-BAT score 3 in paediatric patients, range 1–9; consistently mild in adulthood, median 0). Ex vivo thrombus formation was reduced independent of platelet count. Electron microscopy revealed defective platelet shape maintenance (spheroid morphology, reduced Dmax/Dmin ratios, diminished alpha-granule pools), and proteomics identified reduced alpha-granule and lysosomal proteins alongside imbalanced regulators of actin remodelling and β-tubulin. These findings establish cytoskeletal disruption and granule biogenesis defects as the dual pathophysiological basis of haemostatic impairment in ETV6-RT — mechanistic data that may guide future targeted therapy development.

## X. Iron Deficiency & Nutritional Anemias

Two substantive papers address iron biology and clinical management this week. Leal et al. (Blood Advances) demonstrated that halofuginone (HF), an inhibitor of heparan sulfate (HS) biosynthesis, suppresses basal and BMP6-induced hepcidin expression in human hepatoma (Hep3B) cells in a dose- and time-dependent manner, with the effect absent in HS-deficient (EXT1⁻/⁻) cells[^4]. In vivo, HF reduced hepcidin expression in an iron-overload mouse model (8.3 g/kg carbonyl iron), with the effect absent in mice with impaired liver HS sulfation (Ndst1f/fAlbCre+), confirming HSPG-mediated hepcidin suppression. HF also decreased hepcidin expression in acute inflammation mouse models. These data establish HF as a mechanistically validated candidate oral hepcidin antagonist relevant to iron-refractory iron-deficiency anemia (IRIDA) and anemia of chronic inflammation. Translation to clinical trials requires pharmacokinetic and safety data in humans.

Cool et al. (AJH) published a study on underdosing of intravenous iron in hospitalized patients with iron-deficiency anemia[^23], though quantitative data were not available in the abstract retrieved. This real-world quality improvement report underscores a persistent implementation gap in the management of hospital-acquired and disease-related iron deficiency — relevant to clinical practice at NCKUH and other Taiwanese medical centres where IV iron prescribing practices may not always align with guideline-recommended dosing.

## XI. Thrombosis & Coagulation (VTE, APS, inherited thrombophilia)

This week's most impactful non-malignant hematology paper is the UK multicenter observational study of anticoagulation practices and outcomes in 500 adult patients with thrombotic APS across 20 NHS trusts during 2012–2021 (Crossette-Thambiah et al., Blood Advances)[^1]. This represents the largest multicenter APS observational study published to date. Thrombotic recurrence over the last decade occurred in 34.4% of single-positive, 32.6% of dual-positive, and 39.8% of triple-positive aPL patients, with an overall recurrence rate of 46%. There was no statistically significant difference in recurrence probability based on the number of positive aPL tests (P = 0.82), but lupus anticoagulant positivity was a significantly stronger predictor of recurrence compared with isolated anticardiolipin or anti-β2GPI antibodies (P < 0.01). Patients with arterial thrombosis had a significantly higher probability of recurrence than those with venous thrombosis (P = 0.03). The 10-year overall bleeding rate was 22.0%, with 6.7% experiencing major bleeding. These data highlight that current risk stratification approaches for APS are insufficient and that recurrence remains unacceptably high on vitamin K antagonist therapy, supporting the pursuit of novel adjunctive strategies.

Stevens et al. (Haematologica) performed an important UK Biobank analysis of prothrombotic genetic variants and cancer-associated thromboembolism (CAT) risk across 42,122 cancer patients from 398,053 total participants[^24]. Among seven evaluated thrombophilic variants (ABO, FGG, FXI, FVL, PTM, SLC44A2, TSPAN15), factor V Leiden (FVL) conferred the highest CAT risk (HR 1.81 for one risk allele; HR 5.75 for two risk alleles), followed by SLC44A2 (HR 1.51 for one risk allele, HR 1.53 for two risk alleles). For FVL heterozygotes, the 2-year absolute VTE risk approximately doubled in colorectal cancer (5.17% to 9.26%) and more than doubled in lymphoma (4.16% to 10.59%). Genetic effects were greatest in high-risk cancers including pancreatic and lung malignancies. These findings support the concept that FVL carrier status should inform thromboprophylaxis decision-making in cancer patients — particularly those with lymphoma who are managed at hematology centres.

Regarding HIT, Kanack et al. (AJH) report six cases of ELISA-negative HIT confirmed by PF4-dependent functional testing[^3]. Three patients tested negative on PF4/polyvinylsulfonate ELISA, yet demonstrated PF4-dependent platelet activation sensitive to FcγRIIa blockade and inhibited by high heparin concentrations — the canonical pattern of pathogenic HIT antibodies. An additional three cases were identified by functional screening of 500 ELISA-negative patients. Five of six patients were re-exposed to heparin, precipitating platelet count decreases in all, with one new thrombosis. This series provides compelling evidence that PF4/polyanion ELISA-negative results should not automatically exclude HIT in patients with an intermediate-to-high clinical pre-test probability (4Ts score ≥4), and that reflexive functional testing should be considered in such cases.

Tan et al. (BJH) published outcomes data comparing extended reduced-dose versus full-dose apixaban in cancer-associated VTE[^25], though quantitative results were not available in the abstract captured. This study addresses a clinically relevant question regarding optimal anticoagulation intensity during extended-phase treatment in oncological patients, where balancing recurrence against major bleeding — particularly gastrointestinal hemorrhage — is challenging.

## XIII. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings from this week's literature carry direct relevance to clinical practice in Taiwan. Regarding ITP, the sex-stratified proteomic biomarkers for chronic ITP[^2] and the HLA association data in paediatric ITP[^6] are relevant to NCKUH and CGMH paediatric hematology units, which manage substantial ITP caseloads. Taiwan's National Health Insurance (NHI) currently covers eltrombopag and romiplostim for chronic ITP in adults; extension of coverage to paediatric patients, especially those with HLA profiles associated with chronicity, may warrant future policy advocacy. The concern about myelofibrosis associated with TPO-RA overuse[^7] is particularly relevant for paediatric hematologists.

Regarding PNH, while iptacopan and pegcetacoplan are not yet universally available under NHI in Taiwan (eculizumab is covered under a special NHI approval pathway for PNH patients meeting specific criteria), the indirect treatment comparison data from EHA and the 2-year iptacopan data[^12][^14] will likely influence future NHI dossier submissions. Clinicians at NTUH and CGMH managing PNH patients on C5 inhibitors with residual anemia should monitor these datasets.

For thalassemia, Taiwan's alpha-thalassemia carrier prevalence of approximately 4–5% and beta-thalassemia carrier prevalence of approximately 1–2% make the CONSA haemoglobin variant surveillance data[^19] conceptually relevant — compound HbE/β-thalassemia and Hb H disease are encountered in Southeast Asian immigrant populations now residing in Taiwan, and newborn screening protocols at major medical centres should accommodate these variants.

The APS recurrence data[^1] are directly applicable to rheumatology-hematology co-management practices across Taiwan's major medical centres. Lupus anticoagulant-positive APS patients — who carry the highest recurrence risk — require the most intensive anticoagulant strategies and should not be switched to direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), consistent with current ISTH and EULAR guidance. Rivaroxaban is not recommended for triple-positive APS.

The iron deficiency underdosing report[^23] resonates with known clinical practice patterns in Taiwanese hospitals, where IV iron prescribing for inpatient iron-deficiency anemia — particularly post-surgical, post-partum, and in inflammatory bowel disease patients — may benefit from formal dosing audits.

## XIV. Key Takeaways

This week's non-malignant hematology literature delivers four clinically actionable messages and two important mechanistic advances. The APS multicenter study (N = 500) confirms that thrombotic recurrence rates remain unacceptably high (46% overall) on current vitamin K antagonist therapy, with lupus anticoagulant and arterial thrombosis as the dominant risk markers — challenging clinicians to pursue more individualized, perhaps intensified, strategies in highest-risk subgroups[^1]. ELISA-negative HIT is a real and dangerous diagnostic pitfall: the six-case series from Kanack et al. demonstrates that functional PF4-dependent platelet activation assays should be applied when clinical suspicion remains high despite a negative ELISA, as heparin re-exposure in confirmed ELISA-negative HIT caused thrombocytopenia in five of five re-exposed patients and one new thrombosis[^3]. The sex-stratified proteomic ITP biomarker study[^2] and the HLA-ITP prognostic data[^6] represent genuine advances toward individualized prognosis in paediatric ITP, though external validation is essential before clinical adoption. The halofuginone hepcidin suppression data[^4] offer a mechanistically novel approach to treating iron-restrictive anemias and warrant accelerated preclinical-to-clinical translation given the absence of approved oral hepcidin inhibitors. The voxelotor registry data[^5], while confirmatory of prior trial results, serve as an important post-market safety dataset given the drug's withdrawal from global markets; hemoglobin increments of 0.6–0.8 g/dL were maintained at 54 months in PROSPECT with no increase in acute pain crisis rate. Finally, the TPO-RA myelofibrosis signal in paediatric ITP[^7] warrants heightened pharmacovigilance at centres using these agents in children with suboptimal platelet responses.
