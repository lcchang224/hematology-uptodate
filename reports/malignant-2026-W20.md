## Summary

Five signals dominate this week's literature. **(1)** The ANDROMEDA final survival analysis confirms that daratumumab plus bortezomib/cyclophosphamide/dexamethasone (D-VCd) improves overall survival versus VCd alone in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis at a median follow-up of 61.4 months (OS HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.42–0.90, p = 0.0121), with a hematologic CR rate of 59.5% versus 19.2%.[^1] **(2)** A large real-world UK series of 324 treated patients receiving glofitamab or epcoritamab for relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma demonstrates an ORR of 43% and median OS of 6.9 months (95% CI 4.9–10.8), with ECOG ≥2, elevated LDH, and prior bendamustine within 6 months identified as independent adverse prognostic factors.[^2] **(3)** A comprehensive mutational analysis of 95 adult NUP98-rearranged AML patients reveals a median OS of 15.2 months (95% CI 11.9–20.8), but FLT3 inhibitor use was associated with a 5-year OS of 53.3% (HR 0.45 univariate), approaching intermediate-risk outcomes.[^3] **(4)** A phase 2 study of bomedemstat, a LSD1 inhibitor, in essential thrombocythemia demonstrates platelet response in 77% of evaluable patients at 24 weeks, with variant allele frequency reduction in 85% of evaluable participants.[^4] **(5)** Dynamic monitoring of cell-free EBV-DNA in 710 patients with early-stage NK/T-cell lymphoma identifies four distinct cfEBV-DNA response phenotypes with strikingly different 5-year PFS rates of 94%, 83%, 57%, and 18%, respectively (p for trend < 0.001), establishing a practical risk-stratification framework applicable in Asian centers.[^5]

## I. AML — Acute Myeloid Leukemia

A large cohort study published in *Leukemia* this week characterizes 95 adult patients with NUP98-rearranged AML (NUP98r), the largest such adult series to date.[^3] Patients were young (median age 50 years, IQR 38–64), and the NUP98::NSD1 fusion predominated (54%), occurring in an even younger subgroup (median 41 years) with significantly higher rates of FLT3-ITD (76% vs 18%) and WT1 mutations (50% vs 16%) compared with other NUP98 partner fusions. Overall, median OS was only 15.2 months (95% CI 11.9–20.8) and event-free survival 5.8 months, confirming the adverse biology of this category. Critically, among intensively treated patients, FLT3 inhibitor therapy was associated with an HR of 0.45 in univariate OS analysis, with a 5-year OS of 53.3% for FLT3 inhibitor-treated patients — an outcome approaching ELN intermediate-risk performance. These data provide strong support for reflexive FLT3 inhibitor incorporation in NUP98r AML whenever FLT3-ITD is co-occurring.

A translational study in *Blood* from Uckelmann, Gadrey, and Brunetti elucidates the mechanism by which mutant NPM1 organizes phase-separated nuclear condensates that concentrate transcriptional regulators at active chromatin, sustaining aberrant HOX/MEIS1 transcription.[^6] This framework directly explains the efficacy of menin-KMT2A inhibitors (recently FDA-approved) in NPM1-mutated AML and positions disruption of these transcriptional assemblies as a precision therapeutic strategy. A complementary resistance-focused study in *Blood* demonstrates that PRMT9-mediated arginine methylation promotes venetoclax resistance in AML.[^7] High PRMT9 expression was found in venetoclax-resistant (VEN-R) AML patient-derived xenograft (PDX) cells; PRMT9 inhibition re-sensitized cells by disrupting RNA splicing of ALG13 (downregulating the efflux transporter ABCC1) and suppressing protein synthesis of MCL1. In preclinical VEN-R mouse models, the combination of a PRMT9 inhibitor with venetoclax eradicated AML cells, nominating PRMT9 as a therapeutic target for VEN-resistant disease.

A post-hoc analysis of the QUIWI trial (NCT04107727) published in *Haematologica* evaluated quizartinib added to chemotherapy in 273 newly diagnosed FLT3-ITD–negative AML patients.[^8] Modeling allo-HCT as a time-dependent covariate, quizartinib independently improved OS (HR 0.59, p = 0.008) and DFS (HR 0.67, p = 0.03), whereas allo-HCT itself was not significantly associated with OS benefit (HR 0.91, p = 0.62). Multivariable analysis confirmed quizartinib as an independent favorable factor for OS (HR 0.56, p = 0.046) and DFS (HR 0.60, p = 0.04), with the greatest benefit observed in patients not proceeding to transplant. A chromatin immunoprecipitation–sequencing and RNA-sequencing study of 86 and 357 MDS patients in *Blood* further identified a PU.1-activated epigenomic signature that identifies a subset of low-risk MDS patients with transcriptional profiles similar to high-risk MDS and sAML, SRSF2 mutation enrichment, and significantly reduced overall survival — providing a mechanistic basis for early disease progression in this unexpectedly high-risk group.[^9]

Separately, *Blood Advances* published a validation study of ICC hierarchical classification in 924 patients with secondary AML, demonstrating that MDS/AML with MDR-gene mutation had meaningfully superior median OS compared with AML (24.8 vs 13.6 months, p < 0.0001), underscoring the clinical relevance of blast-count thresholds within ICC categories.[^10]

## II. ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

A mechanistic study in *Blood* identifies DHODH (dihydroorotate dehydrogenase) as a targetable metabolic vulnerability in chemotherapy-resistant B-ALL.[^11] Cells with active mTOR signaling (marked by high phospho-S6) were found to be glucose-dependent, relying on de novo pyrimidine synthesis via CAD phosphorylation. Elevated DHODH expression correlated with relapse and poor event-free survival across multiple B-ALL subtypes. In patient-derived xenograft models, the DHODH inhibitor BAY-2402234 demonstrated efficacy that correlated with pS6 signaling strength across 14 PDX lines, and in vivo DHODH inhibition prolonged survival and reduced leukemia burden in pS6+ B-ALL models. This work provides a clinically actionable framework linking mTOR-active, glucose-dependent clones to pyrimidine synthesis dependency and identifies a candidate therapeutic strategy for relapsed disease.

A multisite retrospective study published in *Blood Advances* evaluated CD19 CAR T-cell outcomes in 308 pediatric and young adult patients with relapsed/refractory B-ALL, stratified by disease distribution at infusion.[^12] The 24-month OS was 71.5% (95% CI 66.0–77.6) for isolated bone marrow disease, 66.7% (95% CI 49.3–90.2) for active non-CNS extramedullary disease (EMD), and 57.0% (95% CI 42.6–76.3) for active CNS disease (p = 0.032). Concurrent high bone marrow disease burden markedly worsened outcomes in both EMD (OS 41.7% vs 100% for low burden, p = 0.005) and CNS disease (OS 33.3% vs 92.3%, p = 0.001) cohorts. These data suggest that pre-infusion disease burden stratification should inform treatment planning and potentially prompt enhanced cytoreduction prior to CAR T-cell infusion in high-burden extramedullary disease.

## III. CML — Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

A translational study published in *Blood* characterizes circPCMTD1, a protein-coding circular RNA in BCR/ABL1-positive myeloid leukemias.[^13] Depletion of circPCMTD1 strongly inhibited leukemic cell proliferation by inducing aberrant DNA damage response (DDR) activation, increased genotoxic stress, and impaired replication fork stability. Mechanistically, the protein product cPCMTD1-127aa interacts with BCR/ABL1 and stabilizes the BTR complex (Bloom syndrome protein, TOP3A, RMI1), thereby increasing cellular tolerance to genotoxic stress. In vivo circPCMTD1 knockdown prolonged survival of mice engrafted with BCR/ABL1-positive leukemic cells, establishing circPCMTD1 as a potential therapeutic target in TKI-resistant CML.

A real-world observational subanalysis from the Italian Iclusig registry, published in *Haematologica*, reports long-term safety and efficacy of ponatinib specifically in TKI-intolerant CML patients.[^14] These data are particularly relevant at NCKUH, where compound TKI intolerance increasingly drives sequencing decisions outside of clinical trials.

## IV. CLL / SLL — Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

A review in *Blood Advances* synthesizes available data on venetoclax retreatment in CLL, covering 10 published abstracts or articles.[^15] Across studies, ORR to retreatment ranged from 72% to 100%, and uMRD rates in peripheral blood from 32% to 92%, with median PFS ranging from 23 to 58 months. The median time off venetoclax prior to retreatment ranged from 9 to 90 months across series. Fixed-duration regimens used initially are associated with a lower incidence of BCL2 mutation emergence at relapse compared with continuous therapy, supporting the mechanistic rationale for retreatment. Patient selection criteria — including depth of initial response and time from last venetoclax exposure — are being incorporated into new clinical trial eligibility criteria, and these data are immediately applicable in counseling patients at NCKUH who have completed fixed-duration VenO or VenR.

A phase 2 single-arm prospective trial of ibrutinib monotherapy in 17 treatment-naïve WM patients, published in *Blood* with single-cell multiomics longitudinal profiling (74 sequential bone marrow aspirates over 48 cycles), identified three distinct clonal evolutionary patterns.[^16] The "evolution" pattern (early clone contraction then late clone expansion with increasing genomic complexity) strongly predicted disease progression, while "devolution" correlated with durable response. Within the resistance signature, LYN emerged as a key regulator, and LYN knockdown or inhibition significantly increased WM cell sensitivity to ibrutinib, suggesting LYN inhibition as a rational combination partner. A separate companion article in *Blood* reviewed the evolutionary pruning of the WM clonal tree.[^17]

A phase 2 GIVeRS trial report in *Haematologica* evaluates fixed-duration obinutuzumab–ibrutinib–venetoclax for Richter's transformation, a disease context with extremely limited evidence.[^18] Full numerical results were not available in the abstract; this trial represents one of very few prospective evaluations of triple-targeted therapy in this setting and will be reported in detail as data become available.

## V. MDS — Myelodysplastic Syndromes

The PU.1-activated epigenomic study in *Blood* (Vallelonga et al.) characterizes H3K27ac ChIP-sequencing and RNA-sequencing data across 443 patients (86 ChIP-seq, 357 RNA-seq) with low-risk MDS, high-risk MDS, and secondary AML.[^9] Unsupervised clustering revealed a low-risk MDS subset harboring regulatory and transcriptional profiles indistinguishable from high-risk MDS/sAML. This subset was enriched for SRSF2 mutations, PU.1 genomic occupancy at immune and inflammatory loci, increased T-cell and NK-cell activation signatures, and demonstrated significantly reduced overall survival, greater infectious complications, and elevated cardiovascular event rates. Functional studies confirmed that PU.1 inhibition suppressed MDS cell proliferation and clonogenicity. These findings are clinically actionable: patients with low-risk MDS, SRSF2 mutation, and inflammatory immune signatures warrant closer surveillance and prospective inclusion in trials incorporating epigenetic agents.

A *Haematologica* study of MDS with concomitant SF3B1 mutation and del(5q) characterizes outcomes and treatment responses in this compound molecular-cytogenetic category, and a separate study addresses the distinct in vivo phenotypic consequences of U2AF1 S34F versus Q157R mutations in mice.[^19][^20] These U2AF1 hotspot mutations induced distinct hematopoietic phenotypes, with U2af1 S34F conferring more severe reductions in blood and bone marrow cell counts and reduced stem cell repopulating ability than Q157R, supporting their potential clinical reclassification as separate disease entities rather than equivalent spliceosomal mutations.

## VI. MPN — Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (PV / ET / MF)

A phase 2 trial of bomedemstat, a lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1) inhibitor, in essential thrombocythemia was published in *Blood Advances* this week.[^4] Among 73 treated patients requiring cytoreduction and having inadequate response to or intolerance of ≥1 prior standard therapy, 49 of 64 evaluable patients (77%) achieved a platelet response (≤400 × 10⁹/L) at 24 weeks, and durable platelet responses were seen in 72% (52/72). Importantly, variant allele frequency reduction in CALR, JAK2, or MPL was observed in 39 of 46 evaluable participants (85%) by week 24, suggesting disease-modifying potential. Durable white blood cell count reduction was achieved in 85% of evaluable patients. Grade 3/4 adverse events occurred in 47%, with thrombotic events in 3% and hemorrhagic events in 21% of patients. These efficacy data position bomedemstat as a candidate second-line agent for ET, particularly in patients with inadequate hematologic control or molecular burden on hydroxyurea.

The phase 1 SENTRY/XPORT-MF-034 study published in *Blood Advances* evaluated selinexor (an exportin-1 inhibitor) at 40 or 60 mg once weekly combined with ruxolitinib in 24 JAK inhibitor-naïve myelofibrosis patients.[^21] No dose-limiting toxicities were observed. SVR35 at week 24 was achieved in 79% and TSS50 in 58% of patients in the selinexor 60-mg group, versus 38% and 25% in the 40-mg group. Among patients receiving suboptimal ruxolitinib ≤5 mg twice daily in the 60-mg group, SVR35 was observed in 100% (6/6), highlighting particular promise in patients with cytopenias limiting ruxolitinib dose escalation. Selinexor 60 mg was identified as the recommended phase 3 dose. A companion review in *Leukemia* provides a comprehensive overview of MDM2 inhibitor development across AML and MPN, summarizing lessons from negative phase III trials in AML and emerging positive signals in myelofibrosis, with combination strategies incorporating JAK inhibitors, BCL-2 antagonists, and interferons under active investigation.[^22]

An apixaban versus aspirin pilot thromboprophylaxis trial in JAK2-positive MPN patients is reported in *Haematologica*, providing preliminary feasibility data for a prospective anticoagulation comparison in this high-thrombotic-risk population.[^23]

## VII. DLBCL & Aggressive B-cell Lymphoma

A large real-world UK retrospective analysis of 332 patients across 34 centers receiving glofitamab (n = 219) or epcoritamab (n = 113) for relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma was published in *Haematologica*.[^2] This high-risk cohort had a median of 2 prior lines of treatment, 55% primary refractory disease, 25% ECOG ≥2, and 50% prior CAR-T therapy; 78% were ineligible for pivotal trial enrollment. ORR was 43% and complete response rate 24% overall (43% CRR in trial-eligible patients), with cytokine release syndrome occurring in 28% (predominantly grade 1/2). At a median follow-up of 10.0 months (IQR 5.3–15.0), median PFS was 3.1 months (95% CI 2.5–4.2) and median OS 6.9 months (95% CI 4.9–10.8). For patients not completing cycle 2, 6-month OS was only 4% (95% CI 1–11%). Independent adverse prognostic factors included refractoriness to the prior line of treatment (HR 2.89, 95% CI 1.73–4.81, p = 0.007), elevated LDH (HR 2.62, 95% CI 1.74–3.93, p = 0.001), bendamustine within 6 months (HR 1.62, 95% CI 1.14–2.30, p = 0.007), and ECOG 1 (HR 2.70, 95% CI 1.52–4.79, p = 0.001) or ECOG 2 (HR 6.49, 95% CI 3.47–12.01). Median duration of complete response was not reached. These data provide the largest real-world benchmark for bispecific antibodies in LBCL post-CAR-T and underscore the importance of completing at least two cycles of treatment.

A case report in *Haematologica* describes CD20 re-emergence in a DLBCL patient who experienced CD20-negative relapse after glofitamab, a phenomenon with increasing clinical relevance as CD20-directed bispecifics are used sequentially.[^24]

A retrospective analysis of 584 patients receiving CAR T-cell therapy for large B-cell lymphoma at 6 Australian centers, published in *Blood*, found no significant association between time of infusion and clinical outcomes, including response, toxicity, or survival, after adjustment for confounders.[^25] This addresses the clinically relevant question of circadian timing of infusion, concluding that chronobiology does not meaningfully impact CAR-T efficacy in LBCL.

## IX. Hodgkin Lymphoma & T-cell Lymphoma

The GHSG HD21 ctDNA-MRD data (MRD-2, LymphoVista assay) reported in the previous week's report remain the most recent HL-specific signal. _No additional new HL trial data this week._

The LEO-MER prospective cohort study, published in *Blood Advances*, reports contemporary first-line outcomes in 720 patients with systemic PTCL enrolled between 2002 and 2020 and followed to 2024.[^27] CHOP-based regimens predominated (70%), with 36% receiving CHOP, 23% CHOP plus etoposide, and 5% BV-CHP. Within nodal PTCL, EFS and OS were adversely associated with IPI 2–5 and PIT score 1–4, as well as non-ALCL subtypes. Adding etoposide to CHOP was associated with better OS in ALK-negative ALCL, and BV-CHP showed a trend toward OS improvement in ALCL. Patients failing EFS6 had 5-year subsequent OS of only 12%, and patients failing EFS24 had 17% subsequent OS, demonstrating the magnitude of unmet need in non-ALCL subtypes and reinforcing the priority for clinical trial enrollment in these patients.

A phase 1 trial of the RAdR combination (romidepsin, azacitidine, dexamethasone, lenalidomide) in relapsed/refractory TCL enrolled 26 patients and established the maximum tolerated dose of lenalidomide at 20 mg in this regimen.[^28] Among 9 patients treated at the MTD, ORR was 89% and CR rate 22%, though 1-year PFS was only 14% and OS 66%, confirming that while RAdR can achieve responses in heavily pretreated TCL, it does not produce durable remissions. Its principal utility may be as a bridge to allogeneic transplantation. Grade 3/4 thrombocytopenia and neutropenia occurred in 19% and 21% of cycles respectively.

In cutaneous TCL, the CTCL-PRO-20 questionnaire was validated in 80 patients across 5 domains (self-consciousness, treatment burden, lack of resources, sleep/fatigue, and skin concerns; Cronbach's α = 0.93), providing a disease-specific patient-reported outcomes tool.[^29] SWI/SNF alterations were identified as defining a poor-outcome subgroup in aggressive adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma in a *Haematologica* molecular study.[^30]

A novel preclinical study in *Leukemia* demonstrates that anti-CD117 CAR T-cells efficiently eliminate neoplastic mast cells from SM patient-derived cell lines, iPS-derived MCs, and primary bone marrow samples, and reduced MC expansion in an advSM xenograft model in vivo.[^31] This is the first report of CAR T-cell activity targeting mast cells in advanced systemic mastocytosis, and may represent a future therapeutic avenue for patients progressing on midostaurin or avapritinib.

## X. NK/T-cell Lymphoma (Asia Focus)

Two important publications this week directly address ENKTL, both highly relevant to the NCKUH patient population.

A retrospective analysis of 710 consecutive patients with early-stage ENKTL from 5 Chinese hospitals, published in *Blood Advances*, examined dynamic monitoring of cell-free EBV-DNA (cfEBV-DNA) during asparaginase-based induction chemotherapy followed by radiotherapy.[^5] Pretreatment cfEBV-DNA positivity was present in 68.6% of patients. Using unsupervised clustering of longitudinal cfEBV-DNA trajectories, four distinct response phenotypes were identified: consistently negative, early response, late response, and no response — with profoundly different 5-year PFS rates of 94%, 83%, 57%, and 18% respectively (p for trend < 0.001), and 5-year OS rates of 98%, 91%, 70%, and 33% (p for trend < 0.001). cfEBV-DNA response phenotype remained independently prognostic for PFS and OS in Cox multivariable analyses. These data provide a practical, non-invasive framework for real-time risk stratification during treatment of early-stage ENKTL, directly applicable at institutions such as NCKUH where ENKTL incidence is enriched.

A separate retrospective study from Samsung Medical Center, published in *Blood Advances*, reports long-term outcomes of 40 Gy CCRT with weekly cisplatin followed by systemic chemotherapy in 155 limited-stage ENKTL patients (median follow-up 73 months).[^32] The 5-year PFS was 68.0% and 5-year OS 82.2%. Local control rate was high at 86.5%, with the predominance of distant extranodal relapses (21.3%) rather than local failures. Pre-treatment EBV-DNA positivity correlated with inferior PFS (p = 0.043), and CCRT plus systemic chemotherapy outperformed CCRT alone (p = 0.004). Median SNOT-22 score was 8, indicating minimal sinonasal symptom burden. These data support the feasibility of a 40 Gy reduced-dose CCRT strategy combined with systemic chemotherapy in limited-stage disease, contrasting with the 50–56 Gy recommended by some international guidelines.

## XI. Multiple Myeloma

The ANDROMEDA final survival analysis, published in *Blood* at a median follow-up of 61.4 months, represents the landmark result of the week for myeloma.[^1] In 388 newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis patients (note: AL amyloidosis as a plasma cell disorder closely related to myeloma), updated hematologic CR rates were 59.5% for D-VCd versus 19.2% for VCd (OR 6.03, 95% CI 3.80–9.58, p < 0.0001). Major organ deterioration-free/progression-free survival was significantly superior with D-VCd (HR 0.44, 95% CI 0.31–0.63, p < 0.0001), and most critically, D-VCd demonstrated a statistically significant OS benefit (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.42–0.90, p = 0.0121). Cardiac and renal response rates were 2–3 times higher with D-VCd. Achieving hematologic or cardiac CR was independently associated with improved MOD-PFS and OS. This represents the first OS-positive randomized trial in AL amyloidosis and definitively establishes D-VCd as standard of care.

In the realm of myeloma biology, two complementary studies identify PSMD1 — a key 19S proteasome regulatory particle subunit — as overexpressed in MM and correlating with poor prognosis.[^33] PSMD1 knockdown using siRNA delivered by lipid nanoparticles reduced tumor growth in cell lines, primary patient samples, and xenograft models while sparing normal cells, and overcame both proteasome inhibitor resistance and the protective bone marrow microenvironment. A companion editorial in *Blood* contextualizes PSMD1 targeting as approaching the proteasome "from a new angle," distinct from standard proteasome inhibitors.[^34]

Mezigdomide, a CELMoD targeting IKZF1/IKZF3, was the subject of two mechanistic studies in *Blood* this week. The first, from Chiu et al., demonstrates that IKZF1/IKZF3 are critical transcriptional regulators of T-cell exhaustion: IKZF1 binding to exhaustion gene promoters/enhancers increases with repeated T-cell stimulation, while mezigdomide treatment reduced exhaustion markers, increased pro-inflammatory cytokine expression, and enhanced target-cell killing by alnuctamab (BCMA-targeting bispecific).[^35] The second, from Chen et al., confirms that mezigdomide reduces TIGIT expression in bone marrow T-cells from MM patients, improves cytotoxicity in primary T-cell dysfunction models, and enhances survival in anti-BCMA CAR-T in vivo models by abrogating Ikaros-mediated TIGIT upregulation.[^36] Together, these two reports provide mechanistic justification for ongoing clinical trials combining mezigdomide with T-cell-engaging therapies.

A review in *Leukemia* by Landgren addresses the evolving definition of smoldering multiple myeloma (SMM) in the context of the recent FDA approval of daratumumab for high-risk SMM, challenging the traditional watch-and-wait paradigm and positing that advancing molecular and imaging technologies will progressively reclassify portions of current SMM into early myeloma or benign MGUS.[^37]

A *Blood* "How I Treat" article provides a practical, phase-based framework for preventing and managing infections in patients receiving T-cell bispecific antibodies for NHL or MM, covering pre-treatment screening, antimicrobial prophylaxis, immunoglobulin supplementation, vaccination, and long-term follow-up strategies.[^38] This article is immediately applicable at NCKUH as bispecific antibody use expands in the myeloma and lymphoma programs.

## XII. Transplant & Cellular Therapy (CAR-T / HSCT / GVHD)

An important preclinical study in *Blood* describes ex vivo modulation of hematopoietic cell transplants with the anti-human CD4 antibody MAX.16H5 as a novel immune tolerance-inducing strategy for GVHD prevention.[^39] A single incubation of the graft prolonged survival and reduced GVHD manifestations in NSG mice as effectively as repeated clinically applied immunosuppressants, with impaired TCR signaling and a shift toward an IL-10-dependent regulatory phenotype as the primary mechanism. While preclinical, this approach is conceptually attractive as a one-time ex vivo manipulation eliminating the need for prolonged systemic immunosuppression.

A retrospective analysis of 195 patients receiving axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel) for relapsed/refractory LBCL at a single center, published in *Blood Advances*, compared outcomes before and after implementing an early toxicity management protocol incorporating earlier steroid use and anakinra.[^40] The protocol change significantly reduced CRS duration (adjusted estimate −0.93 days, 95% CI −1.67 to −0.19) and ICANS duration (−2.49 days, 95% CI −4.96 to −0.03) without compromising efficacy or increasing cumulative steroid dose. Day 7 CAR-T expansion was lower in the post-change cohort, likely reflecting earlier steroid intervention, but efficacy outcomes and ICU admission rates were equivalent between cohorts.

A large post-transplant surveillance study in *Blood Advances*, leveraging the BMT Survivor Study across 6,981 survivors, found that TBI and ≥10 years of smoking interact synergistically to increase lung cancer risk after BMT (adjusted subdistribution HR 3.9, 95% CI 3.0–8.9 versus no-TBI never-smokers), while TBI alone without smoking did not independently elevate risk (aHR 0.6, 95% CI 0.2–1.7).[^41] These data support personalized lung cancer surveillance targeting BMT survivors with combined TBI and significant smoking history.

The POT1 long-telomere syndrome characterization study in *Blood* analyzed 51 individuals from 24 families with pathogenic POT1 variants.[^42] UK Biobank participants with pathogenic POT1 variants had a 45% cumulative rate of lymphoid malignancy by age 80 years (HR 8.28, 95% CI 5.29–13.0), spanning ALL and Hodgkin lymphoma in children/young adults to CLL and multiple myeloma in adults. Among asymptomatic carriers aged >60 years, 9 of 10 had cytogenetic or NGS evidence of preclinical clonal lymphoma-associated changes, and 60% of all asymptomatic carriers had B- and/or T-cell clonality by immunophenotyping. These findings establish long telomere length as an inherited pan-lymphoma risk factor with implications for carrier surveillance.
