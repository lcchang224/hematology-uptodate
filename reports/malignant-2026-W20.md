## Summary

Five signals dominate this week's literature. **(1)** The ANDROMEDA trial final survival analysis confirms that daratumumab-VCd (D-VCd) achieves an updated hematologic CR rate of 59.5% versus 19.2% for VCd alone (OR 6.03, 95% CI 3.80–9.58, p <0.0001) in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis, with a statistically significant OS benefit (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.42–0.90, p = 0.0121) at median 61.4 months follow-up.[^1] **(2)** In a UK real-world cohort of 332 patients with relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL), glofitamab and epcoritamab yielded an ORR of 43% and CRR of 24% in a heavily pretreated, predominantly trial-ineligible population; median PFS was 3.1 months (95% CI 2.5–4.2) and median OS was 6.9 months (95% CI 4.9–10.8), with ECOG ≥2 (HR 6.49, 95% CI 3.47–12.01, p <0.001) and refractoriness to prior therapy (HR 2.89, 95% CI 1.73–4.81, p = 0.007) as dominant adverse prognostic factors.[^2] **(3)** A phase 2 study of bomedemstat (LSD1 inhibitor) in essential thrombocythemia (ET) demonstrated a platelet response rate of 77% at 24 weeks with durable allele burden reduction in 85% of evaluable patients.[^3] **(4)** The QUIWI post-hoc analysis reveals that quizartinib improved OS (HR 0.59, p = 0.008) and DFS (HR 0.67, p = 0.03) in newly diagnosed FLT3-ITD–negative AML regardless of allogeneic HCT status, confirming quizartinib as an independent favorable factor in a non–FLT3-ITD population.[^4] **(5)** A PU.1-driven epigenomic study of 86 low-risk MDS patients identifies a subset with high-risk molecular features, SRSF2 enrichment, and significantly inferior OS, potentially warranting reclassification and early therapeutic intervention.[^5]

## I. AML — Acute Myeloid Leukemia

New mechanistic and clinical data this week significantly advance our understanding of both disease biology and treatment strategies in AML. In a *Blood* review, Uckelmann and colleagues provide a conceptual framework for NPM1-mutated AML, describing how mutant NPM1 organizes phase-separated nuclear condensates that concentrate transcriptional regulators at active chromatin and directly sustain the pathogenic HOX/MEIS1 transcriptional program.[^6] This condensate-driven oncogenic transcription model explains the mechanistic basis for menin-KMT2A inhibitor activity recently approved by the FDA in this context, and positions disruption of these nuclear assemblies as a precision therapeutic strategy. A companion translational report in *Blood* identifies PRMT9 as a critical mediator of venetoclax resistance in AML: PRMT9 is overexpressed in venetoclax-resistant (VEN-R) PDX samples, and its inhibition re-sensitizes cells via dual mechanisms — aberrant exon-skipping of ALG13 mRNA downregulating the VEN efflux transporter ABCC1, and suppression of short-lived oncoproteins including MCL1.[^7] Preclinical models combining PRMT9 inhibition with venetoclax showed eradication of VEN-R AML cells, providing a compelling target for clinical development.

The QUIWI phase II post-hoc analysis published in *Haematologica* evaluated the impact of allogeneic HCT as a time-dependent covariate in newly diagnosed FLT3-ITD–negative AML treated with quizartinib versus placebo.[^4] Among 273 randomized patients, approximately 31% in each arm underwent allo-HCT in first composite complete remission. Quizartinib remained independently associated with improved OS (HR 0.56, p = 0.046) and DFS (HR 0.60, p = 0.04) in multivariable analysis regardless of transplant status, with the benefit appearing most pronounced in patients who did not proceed to transplant. In the same issue, a comprehensive mutational profiling study of adult NUP98-rearranged AML was published in *Leukemia*, characterizing the clinical landscape of this heterogeneous entity across a two-center French cohort.[^8] A *Blood Advances* study validated the International Consensus Classification (ICC) hierarchical framework in a cohort of 924 patients with secondary AML/MDS-AML, confirming that genetic categories — particularly TP53-mutated and MDR-cytogenetic abnormality groups — display similar biology and prognosis irrespective of blast count thresholds; MDS/AML with MDR-gene mutations showed significantly improved median OS compared with frank AML (24.8 vs 13.6 months, p <0.0001).[^9]

## II. ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

In B-ALL chemoresistance, a mechanistically important *Blood* study by Liu et al. links active mTOR signaling — marked by high phospho-S6 (pS6+) — to glucose dependency and de novo pyrimidine synthesis as a vulnerability in relapsed disease.[^10] pS6+ cells exhibit high CAD phosphorylation and upregulated DHODH expression, which correlates with relapse risk and poor event-free survival across B-ALL molecular subtypes. The DHODH inhibitor BAY-2402234 killed pS6+ cells in vitro across 14 patient-derived xenografts with IC50 values correlating with pS6 signaling strength, and prolonged survival in pS6+ B-ALL in vivo models, positioning DHODH inhibition as a metabolic synthetic lethality strategy for chemotherapy-resistant B-ALL.

In the cellular therapy space, a multisite retrospective review of 308 children and young adults receiving CD19 CAR-T cells for relapsed/refractory B-ALL from *Blood Advances* reports 24-month OS of 71.5% (95% CI 66.0–77.6) for isolated bone marrow disease, 66.7% (95% CI 49.3–90.2) for non-CNS extramedullary disease (EMD), and 57.0% (95% CI 42.6–76.3) for CNS disease (p = 0.032).[^11] Critically, all five patients with isolated EMD achieved complete response without relapse; however, concurrent high BM disease burden dramatically worsened outcomes in both the EMD and CNS cohorts (EMD-HD OS 41.7% vs EMD-LD 100%, p = 0.005; CNS-HD OS 33.3% vs CNS-LD 92.3%, p = 0.001). A pediatric ALL study in *Haematologica* — already noted in W19 — providing single-cell RNA-seq evidence of poverty-associated steroid resistance and inflammatory myeloid signatures is not repeated here.[^12]

## III. CML — Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

A dedicated *Blood* article by Gambacorti-Passerini reviews the functional biology of circular RNA in CML, providing context for the accompanying mechanistic study by Papaioannou and colleagues, which identifies circPCMTD1 as a protein-coding circRNA with functional relevance in BCR/ABL1-positive myeloid leukemias.[^13] circPCMTD1 depletion inhibited proliferative capacity of BCR/ABL1-positive leukemic cell lines and prolonged survival in engrafted murine models. Mechanistically, circPCMTD1 generates a 127 amino-acid peptide (cPCMTD1-127aa) that interacts with the BCR/ABL1 oncoprotein and the BTR complex (BLM-TOP3A-RMI1), enhancing BTR complex formation and thereby increasing cellular tolerance to genotoxic stress. Targeting this axis suppressed proliferation and clonogenicity, identifying circPCMTD1 as a molecularly tractable vulnerability in CML and potentially other BCR/ABL1-positive leukemias.

A real-world Italian observational study of ponatinib in intolerant CML patients (subanalysis from the Iclusig registry), published in *Haematologica*, provides long-term safety and efficacy data supporting the use of ponatinib as a switching option in patients with treatment-limiting toxicity to prior TKIs.[^14]

## IV. CLL / SLL — Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

A methodologically important analysis in *Leukemia* challenges the universal use of undetectable MRD as a surrogate endpoint for PFS in CLL clinical trials, demonstrating that the correlation between MRD status and PFS is not reliable across all novel therapy settings and patient populations, with important implications for regulatory approval pathways and trial design.[^15] In parallel, a *Blood Advances* review by Castonguay and colleagues summarizes the growing evidence base for venetoclax retreatment in CLL, pooling data from 10 published studies or abstracts.[^16] Reported ORR to retreatment ranged from 72% to 100%, uMRD rates in peripheral blood from 32% to 92%, and median PFS from 23 to 58 months across heterogeneous patient populations that included substantial proportions with IGHV-unmutated or TP53-mutant disease. The depth of initial venetoclax response and time off venetoclax prior to rechallenge emerge as key predictors of retreatment efficacy, supporting a clinically rational re-treatment paradigm especially after fixed-duration regimens.

The GIVeRS phase II trial of fixed-duration obinutuzumab-ibrutinib-venetoclax for Richter's transformation was published in *Haematologica* this week, adding to the emerging literature on triplet immunochemotherapy-free approaches in this high-risk setting.[^17]

## V. MDS — Myelodysplastic Syndromes

A landmark epigenomics study in *Blood* by Vallelonga, Gandolfi, Zampini et al. performs H3K27ac ChIP-seq and RNA-seq on CD34+ bone marrow progenitors from a prospective cohort of 86 (chromatin profiling) and 357 (transcriptomic) patients with MDS across disease stages.[^5] Unsupervised clustering unexpectedly revealed a subset of low-risk MDS patients displaying regulatory and transcriptional profiles resembling high-risk MDS and secondary AML. This subset is characterized by PU.1 occupancy in regions linked to immune and inflammatory responses, increased T-cell and NK cell activation signatures, enrichment of SRSF2 mutations, greater susceptibility to infections and cardiovascular events, and significantly reduced overall survival. Functional validation demonstrated that PU.1 inhibition suppresses MDS cell proliferation and clonogenicity by impairing key transcriptional programs. A companion editorial by Thoms and Pimanda frames PU.1-driven inflammation as a mechanistic driver of low-risk MDS progression.[^18]

Separately, a post-hoc analysis in *Haematologica* addresses the challenging question of classification for MDS patients with isolated del(5q) and concomitant TP53 multihit alterations — a clinically relevant group given contrasting implications for lenalidomide responsiveness versus poor genomic risk — though the full dataset is not yet available.[^19] A registry-based *Haematologica* analysis examines outcomes of patients developing MDS or AML following solid organ transplantation, a growing clinical challenge given improved solid organ transplant survival and increasing CHIP surveillance.[^20] In *Leukemia*, in vivo characterization of U2AF1 S34F and Q157R hotspot mutations demonstrates that these two spliceosome mutations induce distinct hematopoietic phenotypes in mice — with S34F producing more severe pancytopenia and reduced stem cell repopulating ability — supporting their treatment as separate disease entities rather than equivalent alterations.[^21]

## VI. MPN — Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (PV / ET / MF)

Two new clinical studies this week expand therapeutic options in ET and MF. In a phase 2 trial published in *Blood Advances*, bomedemstat — an oral LSD1 (KDM1A) inhibitor — was evaluated in 73 patients with ET requiring cytoreduction who had inadequate response to or were intolerant of ≥1 standard therapy.[^3] At 24 weeks, 49 of 64 evaluable participants (77%) achieved a platelet response (≤400 × 10⁹/L without new thromboembolic events), and durable platelet reduction was maintained in 72%. Notably, a decrease in mutant allele burden (CALR, JAK2, or MPL) was observed in 39 of 46 (85%) evaluable participants by week 24, a depth of molecular response not commonly seen with hydroxyurea or anagrelide. Grade 3–4 adverse events occurred in 47% and hemorrhagic events in 21%; no deaths were attributable to bomedemstat. This positions bomedemstat as a potentially disease-modifying second-line option in ET.

For myelofibrosis, the phase 1 portion of the SENTRY trial (XPORT-MF-034, NCT04562389) evaluated selinexor — an exportin-1 (XPO1) inhibitor — in combination with ruxolitinib in 24 JAK inhibitor–naïve MF patients.[^22] No dose-limiting toxicities were identified, and selinexor 60 mg once weekly was identified as the recommended phase 3 dose. In the 60-mg cohort, SVR35 was achieved in 79% and TSS50 in 58% at week 24; among patients with suboptimal ruxolitinib exposure (≤5 mg twice daily), SVR35 was 100% (6/6) and TSS50 was 75% (3/4). A complementary *Leukemia* review comprehensively evaluates MDM2 inhibitors across myeloid neoplasms, noting that recent myelofibrosis studies demonstrate clinically meaningful spleen, symptom, and molecular responses in TP53 wild-type settings, while early-phase AML trials with MDM2 inhibitors have not yet matured into positive phase III results.[^23] A pilot *Haematologica* trial comparing apixaban versus aspirin for thromboprophylaxis in JAK2-positive MPN also reports this week, adding early randomized data to a question previously addressed only by retrospective series.[^24]

## VII. DLBCL & Aggressive B-cell Lymphoma

The most substantial new dataset in aggressive lymphoma this week is the UK real-world analysis of glofitamab and epcoritamab in relapsed/refractory LBCL, published in *Haematologica*.[^2] Across 34 UK centres and 332 patients (219 glofitamab, 113 epcoritamab), the cohort was notably high-risk: 55% had primary refractory disease, 25% had ECOG ≥2, 50% had received prior CAR-T therapy, and 78% were ineligible for the pivotal trials on which regulatory approval was based. The ORR was 43% and CRR 24% (rising to 43% in trial-eligible patients), with median PFS 3.1 months (95% CI 2.5–4.2) and median OS 6.9 months (95% CI 4.9–10.8) at median 10-month follow-up. Among patients completing cycle 2, 6-month OS was 4% versus substantially higher in those completing treatment. Dominant adverse prognostic factors in multivariate analysis included refractoriness to the prior line (HR 2.89, p = 0.007), elevated LDH (HR 2.62, p = 0.001), recent bendamustine exposure within 6 months (HR 1.62, p = 0.007), and ECOG 2 performance status (HR 6.49, p <0.001). CRS occurred in 28%, predominantly grade 1/2 (91%). These data provide important real-world benchmarks for outcomes in the post-CAR-T bispecific setting and highlight the need for patient selection strategies to optimize benefit.

A separate *Haematologica* case report documents CD20 re-emergence in a patient with DLBCL who experienced a CD20-negative relapse after glofitamab, underscoring antigen loss as a clinically important resistance mechanism to CD20-directed therapies.[^25]

## VIII. Indolent Lymphoma (FL, MCL, MZL)

Waldenström macroglobulinemia (WM) receives substantial new mechanistic attention this week. A prospective phase 2 single-cell multiomics study of ibrutinib monotherapy in 17 treatment-naïve WM patients, published in *Blood*, performed serial bone marrow profiling across 74 sequential aspirates spanning 48 treatment cycles.[^26] Three distinct clonal evolutionary patterns were identified: "evolution" (early contraction, late expansion, increasing genomic complexity — strongly associated with disease progression), "devolution" (early expansion, late contraction, genomic simplification — correlated with durable response), and "no evolution" (stable clonal architecture). Transcriptomic profiling of resistant clones enabled development of the Waldenström ibrutinib prediction (WIP) score, which predicted treatment response at baseline. Within the WIP signature, LYN kinase emerged as a key resistance regulator — LYN knockdown or inhibition significantly increased WM cell sensitivity to ibrutinib, supporting a rational combination strategy. Additionally, GZMB+ CD8+ effector-memory T cells coexisted with tumor evolution in progressive disease patients, exhibiting impaired cytotoxic programs and elevated PDCD1 expression. A companion editorial by Blaney and Morgan contextualizes these findings within the broader WM clonal evolution framework.[^27]

## IX. Hodgkin Lymphoma & T-cell Lymphoma

The LEO-MER prospective cohort study, published in *Blood Advances*, reports patterns of care and outcomes for 720 patients with systemic peripheral T-cell lymphoma (PTCL) enrolled across two related US cohort studies from 2002 to 2024.[^28] CHOP-based regimens dominated first-line therapy (70%), with 14% undergoing consolidative autologous SCT. Among nodal PTCL, EFS and OS were adversely associated with IPI 2–5 and non-ALCL subtypes. Patients failing EFS6 and EFS24 had 5-year subsequent OS of only 12% and 17%, respectively — underscoring the profound unmet need after early failure. Notably, adding etoposide to CHOP was associated with better OS in ALK-negative ALCL; BV-CHP showed a trend toward OS improvement in ALCL overall. These data reinforce the rationale for clinical trial enrollment with targeted agents for non-ALCL PTCL subtypes.

A *Haematologica* genomics study identifies SWI/SNF complex alterations as defining a subgroup with poorer outcomes among patients with aggressive adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (ATLL), a finding of particular relevance to regions endemic for HTLV-1.[^29] For cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL), the CTCL-PRO-20 — a novel 20-item patient-reported outcomes questionnaire — was validated in a preliminary cross-sectional study demonstrating high internal consistency (Cronbach's α = 0.93) across five components: self-consciousness, treatment burden, lack of resources, sleep/fatigue, and skin concerns.[^30]

In the NK/T-cell lymphoma space (see Section X for full discussion), a *Blood Advances* study from Samsung Medical Center reports long-term outcomes of reduced-dose (40 Gy) concurrent chemoradiotherapy in limited-stage ENKTL.

## X. NK/T-cell Lymphoma (Asia Focus)

A *Blood Advances* retrospective analysis from Samsung Medical Center reports long-term outcomes of 40 Gy concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CCRT) with weekly cisplatin followed by systemic chemotherapy in 155 patients with newly diagnosed limited-stage extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma (ENKTL), nasal type, treated between 2000 and 2023.[^31] At a median follow-up of 73 months, 5-year PFS was 68.0% and 5-year OS was 82.2%. Pretreatment EBV DNA positivity correlated significantly with inferior PFS (p = 0.043). Importantly, CCRT plus chemotherapy achieved superior PFS compared with CCRT alone (p = 0.004). Local control remained high at 86.5%, with the majority of relapses (21.3%) occurring at extranodal distant sites rather than regionally — supporting the feasibility of a reduced-dose RT strategy (below the guideline-recommended 50–56 Gy) combined with systemic therapy without compromising locoregional disease control. Sinonasal morbidity was minimal (median 22-item SNOT score 8). These data are highly relevant to Asian centers including those in Taiwan, where ENKTL is disproportionately prevalent, as reduced-dose CCRT may offer comparable oncologic control with reduced late toxicity to critical structures.

## XI. Multiple Myeloma

This week's myeloma literature is dominated by several converging advances. The ANDROMEDA final survival analysis, published in *Blood*, represents the most mature randomized evidence in AL amyloidosis to date and is the most clinically impactful finding of the week.[^1] At a median follow-up of 61.4 months, D-VCd achieved an updated hematologic CR rate of 59.5% versus 19.2% for VCd (OR 6.03, 95% CI 3.80–9.58, p <0.0001), with a shorter median time to hematologic CR (67.5 vs 85.0 days). Major organ deterioration–PFS was significantly improved (HR 0.44, 95% CI 0.31–0.63, p <0.0001), as was OS (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.42–0.90, p = 0.0121). Cardiac and renal response rates were 2–3 times higher with D-VCd. These mature overall survival data conclusively establish D-VCd as the standard of care for newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis.

Two mechanistically complementary studies address T-cell exhaustion and its reversal by mezigdomide in myeloma. Chiu and colleagues in *Blood* demonstrate that IKZF1 and IKZF3 are critical regulators of T-cell exhaustion through direct promoter/enhancer binding, with IKZF1 binding increasing exhaustion gene transcription while repressing cytokine genes.[^32] Mezigdomide treatment of exhausted T cells decreased exhaustion markers, increased pro-inflammatory cytokine expression, and enhanced target cell killing with the BCMA-directed bispecific alnuctamab. A companion paper by Chen et al. demonstrates reduced dysfunctional T-cell populations expressing TIGIT upon mezigdomide treatment in bone marrow samples from MM patients, and shows via ATAC-seq, ChIP-seq, HiC, and RNA-seq that IKZF1 (Ikaros) directly regulates TIGIT transcription; mezigdomide enhanced survival outcomes from anti-BCMA CAR-T therapy in vivo.[^33] Together, these studies provide a strong mechanistic rationale for combining mezigdomide with T-cell engagers or CAR-T to overcome exhaustion-mediated resistance.

A translational *Blood* study by Du and colleagues identifies PSMD1 — a 19S proteasome regulatory particle subunit — as overexpressed in MM and correlated with poor prognosis.[^34] PSMD1-targeting siRNA delivered via lipid nanoparticles reduced tumor growth in MM xenograft models, prolonged survival, overcame proteasome inhibitor resistance, and bypassed the protective bone marrow microenvironment, positioning PSMD1 as a novel proteasome subunit target that circumvents known bortezomib/carfilzomib resistance mechanisms.

For smoldering multiple myeloma (SMM), Landgren's perspective in *Leukemia* reviews the FDA approval of daratumumab for high-risk SMM as a historic therapeutic shift, discussing how emerging molecular technologies may progressively reclassify portions of today's SMM population as either early active myeloma or benign monoclonal gammopathy.[^35] A comprehensive *Blood* review of dual-antigen-targeting T-cell immunotherapies in MM by van de Donk, Sonneveld, and Einsele discusses emerging data with BCMA×GPRC5D trispecific antibodies (ramantamig, ISB 2001), combination bispecific strategies (teclistamab plus talquetamab), and dual-targeting CAR-T products, noting that none of the dual-targeting CAR-T products has yet demonstrated clear superiority over ciltacabtagene autoleucel targeting BCMA alone.[^36]

A practical "How I Treat" article in *Blood* by Rejeski, Banerjee, and Hill provides an evidence-informed phase-based framework for infection prevention in patients receiving bispecific antibodies for B-cell malignancies or MM, addressing antimicrobial prophylaxis, immunoglobulin supplementation, vaccination strategies, and surveillance protocols — highly relevant as bispecific antibodies enter combination and frontline settings.[^37]

## XII. Transplant & Cellular Therapy (CAR-T / HSCT / GVHD)

A *Blood* preclinical study introduces anti-CD4 antibody (MAX.16H5) ex vivo modulation of hematopoietic transplants as a novel GVHD prevention strategy.[^38] A single short-term incubation of the graft with MAX.16H5 impaired T-cell receptor signaling and shifted the immunophenotype toward an IL-10–dependent regulatory phenotype, significantly reducing CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell activation. In NSG mice, single-dose MAX.16H5 modulation prolonged survival and reduced GVHD manifestation as effectively as repeated conventional immunosuppressants, presenting a potentially simpler ex vivo approach to GVHD prophylaxis.

For axi-cel toxicity management, a retrospective analysis from *Blood Advances* covering 195 patients at six Australian centers — note: not the ruxolitinib/ciltacabtagene autoleucel diarrhea series reported in W19 — evaluated an early intervention protocol incorporating anakinra for axi-cel CRS/ICANS in LBCL.[^39] Compared with the pre-protocol cohort, the modified protocol produced significant reductions in both CRS duration (adjusted estimate −0.93 days, 95% CI −1.67 to −0.19) and ICANS duration (−2.49 days, 95% CI −4.96 to −0.03), without increases in cumulative steroid dose, ICU admissions, or hospitalization length, and with preserved efficacy outcomes — establishing anakinra as a safe alternative to tocilizumab in this setting.

A *Leukemia* preclinical study introduces anti-CD117 CAR-T cells as a potential therapeutic strategy for advanced systemic mastocytosis (advSM), demonstrating efficient lysis of SM-related cell lines, iPS-derived mast cells, and patient-derived neoplastic BM cells in vitro, and inhibition of MC expansion in immunocompromised advSM mouse models.[^40] This is the first immune therapy approach developed specifically for advSM, a disease currently lacking effective non-transplant systemic options outside of midostaurin and avapritinib.

A *Blood Advances* analysis from the BMT Survivor Study confirms that total body irradiation (TBI) and smoking interact synergistically to increase subsequent lung cancer risk after BMT (p <0.0001): TBI-exposed ≥10-year smokers had an asHR of 3.9 (95% CI 3.0–8.9) compared with no-TBI never-smokers, supporting personalized surveillance protocols for BMT survivors.[^41]

## XIV. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings from this week's report have direct relevance to clinical practice at NCKUH and across major Taiwanese hematology centers. Regarding AL amyloidosis, the ANDROMEDA final OS analysis definitively establishes D-VCd as standard of care; subcutaneous daratumumab is available in Taiwan under NHI coverage for myeloma indications, though AL amyloidosis-specific approval should be confirmed with local regulatory status. Taiwanese centers including NTUH, CGMH, and NCKUH treating AL amyloidosis patients should note the nearly threefold higher hematologic CR rate and significant OS benefit with D-VCd.

For NK/T-cell lymphoma, the Samsung Medical Center ENKTL data on 40 Gy CCRT are highly clinically relevant to Taiwan, where ENKTL is significantly more prevalent than in Western populations, paralleling the Korean and Chinese epidemiologic pattern. The demonstration of durable 5-year OS of 82.2% with the reduced-dose CCRT strategy — and the predominance of distant over locoregional relapse — supports consideration of this approach at NCKUH radiation oncology, where proximity to ENKTL expertise and CCRT facilities makes this directly applicable. Pretreatment EBV DNA monitoring, which predicted inferior PFS (p = 0.043), is already standard practice in most Taiwanese institutions.
