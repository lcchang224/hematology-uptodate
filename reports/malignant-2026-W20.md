## Summary

Five new signals dominate this week. **(1)** The phase 2 E4412 Intergroup trial of BV/Nivo versus BV/Ipi/Nivo in relapsed/refractory classic Hodgkin lymphoma did not meet its primary endpoint of superior CR rate for the triplet (64.7% vs 70.3%, p = 0.29); however, among the 66 patients who were progression-free after first scan and elected to defer SCT, BV/Ipi/Nivo yielded a 36-month PFS of 73.0% versus 45.8% for BV/Nivo (HR 0.45, 95% CI 0.19–1.08, p = 0.03), generating a hypothesis-generating signal for chemotherapy-free consolidation.[^1] **(2)** The GIVeRS phase II trial of fixed-duration obinutuzumab–ibrutinib–venetoclax for Richter's transformation has been published in *Haematologica*, providing prospective data for this notoriously difficult-to-treat entity.[^2] **(3)** A *Leukemia* meta-analysis of 43 CLL trials (n = 9,628) demonstrates that MRD is a weak trial-level surrogate for PFS under BTKi-based regimens (Spearman rho = –0.05, R² = 0.03), challenging regulatory use of MRD as a primary endpoint in this context.[^3] **(4)** Anti-CD117 CAR T-cells efficiently lysed neoplastic mast cells from advanced systemic mastocytosis (advSM) patient-derived models and suppressed expansion in immunocompromised mice, representing the first preclinical CAR T-cell framework specifically developed for advSM.[^4] **(5)** The phase 1/2 NXTAGE trial of NXT007, a next-generation FVIIIa-mimetic bispecific antibody, demonstrated zero annualized treated bleed rate at maintenance doses ≥0.70 mg/kg SC every 4 weeks in hemophilia A without inhibitors, with predicted FVIII-equivalent activity reaching nonhemophilic levels (≥40 IU/dL) from cohort B2 onwards.[^5]

## I. AML — Acute Myeloid Leukemia

A *Blood Advances* study applied a novel long-read genotyping pipeline called LOTR-Seq — capable of simultaneously genotyping up to 30 genes across the full length of expressed transcripts — to 9,075 single cells from eight patients with chronic-phase MPN and 5,016 cells from four patients with post-MPN AML.[^6] By linking mutations at JAK2V617F, IDH1/2, TP53, SRSF2, and U2AF1 loci to the single-cell transcriptome, the authors identified nine mutated loci across six genes in post-MPN AML cases and demonstrated that individual mutations couple to specific transcriptional phenotypes, illuminating discrete mechanisms of clonal evolution in this aggressive transformation setting. The ability to simultaneously genotype low-expression genes such as JAK2 alongside other pathogenic loci represents a methodologic advance over existing short-read approaches.

A *Blood Advances* preclinical study in pediatric KMT2A-rearranged (KMT2A-r) AML identified RAS pathway co-mutations in 43% of 1,750 patients enrolled on Children's Oncology Group trials, a co-mutational pattern associated with lower complete remission rate, inferior event-free and overall survival, and early relapses.[^7] The menin inhibitor revumenib combined with the MEK1/2 inhibitor selumetinib produced synergistic decreases in viability and promoted apoptosis in KMT2A-r/RAS-mutant AML cell lines and patient-derived xenografts, though the combination did not improve overall survival versus menin inhibition alone in vivo, highlighting the need for further optimization before clinical translation.

A *Leukemia* review comprehensively addresses MDM2 inhibitors in myeloid cancers, noting that while early phase III trials in AML were negative, recent myelofibrosis studies demonstrate clinically meaningful spleen, symptom, and molecular responses in TP53 wild-type settings, and that adaptive combinations with JAK inhibitors, BCL-2 antagonists, and interferons may refine future therapeutic strategies.[^8]

## II. ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

A *Blood Advances* retrospective report from the Children's Oncology Group examined asparaginase-associated toxicities (AAT) during induction in 4,925 patients aged 1–30 years enrolled on AALL0232 and AALL0434.[^9] Increased risk for at least one AAT was observed in patients aged ≥10 years (p = 0.002) and in those with both obesity and high BSA (p < 0.0001) but not with high BSA alone. Notably, risks for hyperbilirubinemia, elevated ALT, and thrombosis were all elevated in the obese/high-BSA group (OR 3.5, 95% CI 2.2–5.7; OR 3.3, 95% CI 1.7–6.6; and OR 3.1, 95% CI 1.5–6.5, respectively), yet AATs did not associate with end-of-induction MRD positivity — a clinically reassuring finding for risk-stratification purposes.

A *Leukemia* report describes a reduced-intensity reinduction strategy for children and young persons with relapsed ALL, adding to the evolving evidence base for de-escalated approaches in specific relapse-risk subgroups.[^10] In the context of inherited cancer risk, a *Blood* cohort study of 51 individuals from 24 families with pathogenic POT1 variants demonstrated that lymphoid malignancies were the second most common cancer type (after melanoma), with UK Biobank participants carrying pathogenic POT1 variants showing a cumulative lymphoid malignancy rate of 45% by age 80 (HR 8.28, 95% CI 5.29–13.0).[^11] Diagnoses in children and young adults included ALL and Hodgkin lymphoma, and 60% of asymptomatic carriers aged >65 years showed complete penetrance of B- or T-cell clonality by immunophenotyping, underscoring the importance of surveillance in this hereditary syndrome.

## III. CML — Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

A *Haematologica* case report (first published during the W19 window but noted here for completeness of the CML subsection) describes a myeloid blast phase of CML co-occurring with t(11;12)(p15;q13)/NUP98::HOXC12, a novel NUP98 fusion partner not previously reported in CML blast crisis.[^12] This finding expands the catalogue of NUP98 rearrangements as potential drivers of blast transformation in CML and illustrates the diagnostic value of comprehensive cytogenomic profiling at progression.

## IV. CLL / SLL — Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

A meta-analysis in *Leukemia* systematically evaluated MRD as a surrogate for PFS across 43 CLL trials (n = 9,628), with results that carry immediate regulatory and clinical implications.[^3] While at the individual patient level, detectable MRD was significantly associated with a higher risk of progression or death versus undetectable MRD (HR 3.67, 95% credibility interval 3.34–4.03, p < 0.01), the trial-level correlation between MRD rate and PFS was weak overall (Spearman rho = –0.33, R² = 0.06). The surrogate relationship was particularly poor for BTKi-based therapies (rho = –0.05, R² = 0.03) and venetoclax-based regimens (rho = –0.28, R² = 0.02), whereas MRD of bone marrow in chemoimmunotherapy and/or monoclonal antibody-based trials showed strong trial-level correlation (rho = –0.94, R² = 0.86). For fixed-duration therapy, the correlation approached, but did not consistently exceed, the pre-specified validity threshold. These data strongly suggest that using MRD as the primary endpoint in regulatory submissions for BTKi- or venetoclax-monotherapy trials should be interpreted with considerable caution.

A *Haematologica* phase II study (GIVeRS trial) reported prospective data on fixed-duration obinutuzumab–ibrutinib–venetoclax for Richter's transformation.[^2] Full efficacy data were not available in the abstract at time of reporting, but the triplet backbone — leveraging anti-CD20 antibody, BTK inhibition, and BCL-2 blockade — represents a rationally designed fixed-duration approach to one of the most therapeutically challenging CLL-related complications. The published data from this trial will inform future management paradigms for Richter's transformation beyond the mosunetuzumab series reported in W19.

## V. MDS — Myelodysplastic Syndromes

A *Leukemia* retrospective study addressed the contentious classification issue of MDS with isolated del(5q) and concomitant TP53 multihit alterations (n = 43 cases) versus low-blast MDS with TP53 multihit without isolated del(5q) (n = 68 cases).[^13] Patients with MDS-del(5q)/TP53 multihit had significantly better outcomes than MDS-LB/TP53 multihit comparators: overall survival of 70.2 versus 13.9 months, and time to AML progression of 31.9 versus 7.2 months. The prognostic advantage persisted even when compared with MDS-LB/TP53 multihit cases lacking complex karyotype (OS 70.2 vs 39.9 months; time to AML progression 31.9 vs 11.4 months). These data suggest that TP53 multihit alterations exert substantially attenuated adverse impact in the del(5q) genetic context, raising the question of whether current classification systems adequately capture this prognostic heterogeneity.

A *Haematologica* study characterized the clinical features and treatment outcomes of MDS patients with concomitant SF3B1 mutation and chromosome 5q deletion — a combination that occupies a nosological gray zone between the favorable SF3B1 mutant category and del(5q) MDS.[^14] The intersection of two spliceosome and chromosomal abnormalities in the same patient warrants individualized assessment of IPSS-M risk scores and lenalidomide response kinetics.

A *Blood* "How I Treat" article by Groarke and colleagues provides a framework for integrating somatic alterations — including clonal hematopoiesis signatures specific to immune aplastic anemia, hypoplastic MDS, and inherited bone marrow failure syndromes — into diagnosis, risk stratification, and treatment decisions for hypocellular bone marrow failure.[^15]

In *Leukemia*, comprehensive murine in vivo modeling of U2AF1 S34F and U2AF1 Q157R hotspot mutations reveals that these two recurrently mutated spliceosome variants induce phenotypically distinct hematopoietic abnormalities: U2af1-S34F mice display more severe reduction in blood and bone marrow cell counts and reduced stem cell repopulating ability than U2af1-Q157R mice, with largely non-overlapping target gene expression and splicing profiles in both mouse and human samples.[^16] These findings challenge the clinical practice of grouping U2AF1 S34F and Q157R mutations as interchangeable entities and may have implications for prognostic modeling in MDS.

## VI. MPN — Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (PV / ET / MF)

A pilot trial published in *Haematologica* randomized JAK2-positive MPN patients to thromboprophylaxis with apixaban versus aspirin.[^17] Although full data were not available at time of reporting (abstract only), the head-to-head comparison of a direct oral anticoagulant against aspirin as thromboprophylaxis in JAK2-positive MPN addresses an unresolved clinical question with direct implications for management of polycythemia vera and essential thrombocythemia — conditions in which thrombotic risk is considerable and optimal anticoagulation strategy remains debated.

The *Blood Advances* LOTR-Seq study (covered under AML above) also provides novel single-cell genotypic and transcriptomic insights into clonal evolution from JAK2V617F-driven chronic-phase MPN through blast transformation, with transcriptional phenotypes linked to SRSF2, U2AF1, IDH1/2, and TP53 co-mutations.[^6]

## VII. DLBCL & Aggressive B-cell Lymphoma

A large population-based study in *Blood Advances* analyzed excess nonlymphoma mortality in 58,221 patients with DLBCL diagnosed in England from 1997 to 2020, followed for up to 25 years (median 10.2 years).[^18] The nonlymphoma mortality rate was 58% higher than the general population (SMR 1.58, 95% CI 1.55–1.61), with more than 100 excess nonlymphoma deaths per 10,000 person-years (AER 103.8, 95% CI 98.2–109.4). The AER was highest in the first year (predominantly infectious causes, 29% of AER) and remained significantly elevated beyond 10 years (AER 93.7, 95% CI 80.5–107.3), driven by hematological second malignancies at years 1–4 (38%) and solid tumors thereafter (31–34%). These data contextualize the long-term survivorship burden in DLBCL and support the case for structured survivorship programs addressing infection prophylaxis, second malignancy screening, and cardiovascular disease monitoring.

A retrospective study in *Blood Advances* evaluated polatuzumab vedotin (PV)-based bridging therapy in 200 LBCL patients from the CART-SIE prospective multicenter cohort.[^19] PV-BR (PV + rituximab + bendamustine; n = 122) versus PV-R (PV + rituximab; n = 78) yielded similar objective response rates to bridging (52.5% vs 49.4%, p = 0.775), median PFS (13.4 vs 7.4 months, p = 0.556), and median OS (not reached vs 29.0 months, p = 0.954) following CAR T infusion. However, PV-BR was associated with higher hematological toxicity after bridging (grade ≥3: 16.1% vs 6.5%, p = 0.048) and higher rates of post-CAR T neurotoxicity (31.1% vs 15.4%, p = 0.019), favoring PV-R as a well-tolerated alternative bridging regimen.

A *Blood* retrospective analysis of 584 patients receiving CAR T-cell therapy for LBCL at six Australian centers found no association between time of infusion and outcome after adjusting for confounders.[^20] This large dataset effectively refutes a clinically relevant chronobiology hypothesis and supports operational flexibility in CAR T-cell infusion scheduling.

In CNS lymphoma, a *Blood Advances* study reported that glofitamab crosses the blood-brain barrier and exhibits activity in both primary and secondary CNS lymphoma, providing proof-of-concept for bispecific antibody therapy in this sanctuary-site disease.[^21]

## VIII. Indolent Lymphoma (FL, MCL, MZL)

A *Blood* translational study characterized the interaction of DC-SIGN (expressed on interfollicular macrophages and follicular dendritic cells) with surface immunoglobulin oligomannose-type glycans (sIg-Mann), a tumor-specific post-translational modification in follicular lymphoma (FL).[^22] DC-SIGN engagement induced FL cell adhesion to VCAM-1 via BCR-proximal kinases without initiating apoptosis in vitro, and antibody blockade of the sIg-Mann/DC-SIGN interaction inhibited adhesion and survival of primary FL cells. The findings delineate a tumor-microenvironment retention mechanism and propose anti-DC-SIGN antibody blockade as a novel therapeutic approach to displace FL cells from their adaptive niche.

A *Blood* case report describes classic Hodgkin lymphoma with IGH::CCND1 rearrangement, providing evidence of clonal relationship to a preceding mantle cell lymphoma — a rare but important diagnostic entity that expands understanding of lymphoma transformation biology.[^23]

## IX. Hodgkin Lymphoma & T-cell Lymphoma

The phase 2 E4412 Intergroup trial results for relapsed/refractory classic Hodgkin lymphoma, published in *Blood*, provide this week's most clinically relevant Hodgkin lymphoma dataset.[^1] Among 147 patients aged ≥12 years randomized to BV/Nivo versus BV/Ipi/Nivo (132 included in primary efficacy analysis), the CR rate was 64.7% (95% CI 52.2–75.9%) for BV/Nivo and 70.3% (95% CI 57.6–81.1%) for BV/Ipi/Nivo, failing to meet the pre-specified primary endpoint of CR superiority for the triplet (one-sided p = 0.29). Median survival follow-up was 38.0 months (IQR 32.6–48.1). Overall PFS did not differ significantly between arms (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.39–1.57, one-sided p = 0.24). Of 58 patients who proceeded to SCT, 36-month PFS from SCT exceeded 90% in both arms. The most clinically provocative finding emerged among 66 progression-free patients who did not undergo SCT: those on BV/Ipi/Nivo achieved 36-month PFS of 73.0% (95% CI 54.5–85.0%) versus 45.8% (95% CI 26.3–63.4%) for BV/Nivo (HR 0.45, 95% CI 0.19–1.08, one-sided p = 0.03), generating a hypothesis that the triplet may offer durable disease control for patients deferring or avoiding transplant — a finding warranting prospective validation. Grade 3 rash was substantially higher with the triplet (24.6% vs 9.2%).

A *Haematologica* study found that SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex alterations define a subgroup with poorer outcomes among patients with aggressive forms of adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (ATL/L), adding to the emerging landscape of epigenetic drivers of this HTLV-1-associated lymphoma with particular geographic relevance in East Asia.[^24]

A *Blood Advances* single-cell RNA sequencing study compared lymphomatoid papulosis (LyP) with advanced-stage CTCL (aCTCL), revealing that LyP top clones consistently exhibit a hyperactivated cytotoxic phenotype — enriched for TNF-α, IL-2/STAT5, interferon-γ, hypoxia, and complement activation pathways — whereas aCTCL variants lack this signature.[^25] SPP1+ macrophages with M1-associated markers were prominent in LyP but largely absent in aCTCL, suggesting a cytotoxic, type 1-skewed tumor microenvironment as a mediator of spontaneous regression in LyP, with potential immunomodulatory therapeutic implications.

A *Haematologica* study developed and validated the CTCL-PRO-20, a novel 20-item patient-reported outcomes instrument for CTCL with high internal consistency (Cronbach's α = 0.93), covering five dimensions: self-consciousness, treatment burden, lack of CTCL resources, sleep/fatigue, and skin concerns.[^26]

## XI. Multiple Myeloma

A *Blood* review by van de Donk and colleagues comprehensively addresses dual-antigen–targeting T-cell immunotherapies in MM, synthesizing current evidence on BCMA × GPRC5D bispecific combinations, trispecific antibodies (ramantamig [BCMA×GPRC5D]; ISB 2001 [BCMA×CD38]), and dual-targeting CAR T-cells.[^27] The combination of teclistamab and talquetamab appears to demonstrate enhanced anti-MM activity compared with either single-antigen bispecific antibody used in similar patient populations. The authors note that no dual-targeting CAR T-cell product has yet demonstrated clear superiority over cilta-cel for BCMA-single target, and argue that definitive comparison will require large phase III trials with a target-switch design.

A *Leukemia* perspective by Landgren provides a thought-provoking framework for how the recent FDA approval of daratumumab for high-risk smoldering MM (SMM) — the first validated early intervention in this space — challenges the traditional watch-and-wait paradigm and will likely reshape the nosological boundary between SMM and early active MM over the coming decade.[^28]

A *BJH* case series addresses retreatment with talquetamab and pomalidomide in triple-class–BCMA–GPRC5D-refractory myeloma, an emerging clinical scenario given the sequential use of BCMA-directed and GPRC5D-directed therapies.[^29]

A *Blood* "How I Treat" article on plasma cell leukemia (PCL) synthesizes current evidence and illustrates practical treatment approaches, highlighting that PCL remains largely studied via extrapolation from MM trials from which PCL patients have been predominantly excluded, and arguing for dedicated prospective trials.[^30]

A pooled analysis in *Blood Advances* of infection outcomes from the MAIA and ALCYONE trials (daratumumab-based frontline therapy in transplant-ineligible NDMM) demonstrated that incidence of grade 3/4 infections was higher with D-Rd/D-VMP versus Rd/VMP (36.9% vs 22.4%), though exposure-adjusted incidence rates were generally comparable between groups.[^31] The highest infection incidence occurred within the first 6-month interval, and treatment discontinuation due to infection was approximately 2% across all groups. These data support heightened vigilance for early infectious complications and underscore the importance of infection prophylaxis protocols during the induction phase.

A *Blood Advances* article reports the *Blood Advances* perspective on the immune determinants of infection risk in myeloma (Ainley and Yong), contextualizing the above pooled infection data within the broader framework of myeloma-associated immunoparesis.[^32]

## XII. Transplant & Cellular Therapy (CAR-T / HSCT / GVHD)

A *Blood* preclinical study identified XCR1+ conventional dendritic cells (cDC) — not plasmacytoid DCs — as the principal mediators of Th1-driven gut GVHD, while CD11b+ cDC mediated Th17-dependent gut aGVHD via ALDH1A1-dependent signaling.[^33] Selective depletion of donor XCR1+ cDC attenuated lethal GVHD in murine BMT models, and the pan-ALDH1 inhibitor WIN18446 improved survival by blocking CD11b+ cDC without affecting XCR1+ cDC, demonstrating that subtype-specific dendritic cell targeting represents a mechanistically rational strategy for gut GVHD prevention. These preclinical findings support the development of clinically translatable dendritic cell subset–directed interventions.

A *Blood Advances* study demonstrated that a VLA-4 agonist significantly promotes donor HSC engraftment and long-term hematopoietic reconstitution by enhancing self-renewal capacity in allogeneic transplantation and serial xenotransplantation mouse models, while also augmenting T-cell differentiation from HSCs without exacerbating acute GVHD.[^34] The VLA-4 agonist (designated A2) activated ERK1/2 phosphorylation as the downstream mechanistic mediator, presenting this pathway as a candidate clinical target for improving engraftment and early cellular immune reconstitution after allo-HSCT.

A *Blood Advances* study reported safety and efficacy of the allogeneic CD19-directed CAR T-cell product CTX110 in 63 heavily pretreated r/r B-cell NHL patients.[^35] Among 57 patients whose first infusion was dose level ≥3 (30–60 × 10⁷ CAR T-cells), objective and complete response rates were 65% and 39%, respectively. Among 22 patients achieving CR, five (23%) maintained ongoing CR at data cutoff (median follow-up 34 months). A two-infusion regimen on days 1 and 35 appeared to prolong response duration compared to single infusion (HR 0.65). CRS was reported in 54% of treated patients and ICANS in 13%, with grade ≥3 CRS and ICANS each occurring in 2 patients. These data support the clinical viability of off-the-shelf allogeneic CAR T-cell therapy in LBCL, with the two-infusion approach meriting further phase III evaluation.

A preclinical study in *Leukemia* validated anti-CD117 CAR T-cells as a novel immunotherapeutic strategy for advanced systemic mastocytosis (advSM).[^4] Anti-CD117-CAR T-cells efficiently lysed SM-related human MC cell lines, iPS cell-derived MCs from SM patients, and neoplastic bone marrow cells from advSM patients in vitro. In immunocompromised mice engrafted with an advSM-like MC line, repetitive CAR T-cell application inhibited MC expansion. CD117 is highly expressed on neoplastic MCs and exceeds expression levels on hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells — a differential expression pattern that underpins the selectivity of this approach. No prior immune therapy has been clinically validated for advSM, making this a potentially transformative preclinical development.

A *Blood Advances* editorial from Boiko and Carpenter addresses the rationale for Treg-boosting strategies in chronic GVHD, providing context for the evolving role of regulatory T-cell augmentation as a therapeutic principle.[^36]

A *Blood* paper on FGFR1-rearranged myeloid/lymphoid neoplasms (M/LN-FGFR1) by Vannucchi and colleagues summarizes the data supporting pemigatinib approval in this rare disease, where conventional therapy yields dismal survival and allogeneic SCT remains the only curative option.[^37] The review articulates pemigatinib's unprecedented efficacy as a targeted bridge to transplantation in a genetically defined, previously treatment-refractory population.

## XIV. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings this week carry direct relevance for hematology practice in Taiwan. The E4412 BV/Ipi/Nivo data are of interest given that brentuximab vedotin received NHI coverage in Taiwan for relapsed/refractory cHL following ASCT or ASCT-ineligibility; the combination with checkpoint inhibitors is not yet NHI-reimbursed but may inform bridging or transplant-deferral strategies at NTUH, VGH, CMUH, and CGMH.

The SWI/SNF mutation findings in aggressive ATL/L are directly pertinent to Taiwan, where HTLV-1 seroprevalence reaches 1–3% in certain indigenous populations in eastern Taiwan and where ATL/L is a recognized, though relatively uncommon, clinical problem. Identification of SWI/SNF alterations as prognostic drivers may assist in risk stratification at centers with active ATL/L programs.

The GIVeRS Richter's transformation trial provides the first prospective triplet data for a condition estimated to occur in 2–8% of CLL patients. CLL is managed across all major hematology centers in Taiwan under ibrutinib NHI coverage (second-line indication), and Richter's transformation remains one of the most challenging clinical scenarios without established second-line standard-of-care.

The CTX110 allogeneic CAR T-cell data are relevant to Taiwan's growing CAR T-cell program: unlike autologous products (currently available at select centers including NTUH and CGMH), allogeneic products could dramatically reduce manufacturing lead time and cost — a priority consideration for rapidly progressive lymphoma patients in the Taiwanese healthcare context.

The MDM2 inhibitor review noting efficacy signals in MF is relevant for centers managing MPN patients with TP53 wild-type myelofibrosis who have inadequate responses to ruxolitinib or fedratinib, as MDM2 inhibitor clinical trials remain accessible internationally for this patient population.

## XV. Key Takeaways

The week's most practice-informing finding is the MRD surrogate endpoint analysis in CLL, which has direct implications for trial design and regulatory submissions across Asia-Pacific oncology cooperative groups including Taiwan's hematology clinical trial networks. The E4412 Hodgkin lymphoma data provide the largest randomized dataset yet addressing checkpoint inhibitor triplet induction in relapsed/refractory cHL, and while the primary endpoint was negative, the SCT-deferral subset analysis generates a hypothesis warranting prospective investigation.
