## Summary

The five most clinically actionable signals this week are as follows. First, the ANDROMEDA final survival analysis (median follow-up 61.4 months) confirms that daratumumab-VCd achieves an overall survival benefit over VCd alone in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis (HR 0.62; 95% CI 0.42–0.90; p = 0.0121), representing the first phase III trial to demonstrate an OS advantage in this disease — a landmark endpoint previously elusive[^1]. Second, a Blood study characterizing the epigenetic regulation of heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) identifies the histone demethylase JMJD1C as a novel regulator of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production, with JMJD1C-deficient B cells showing transcriptomic overlap with HIT patient B cells, providing the first mechanistic link between epigenetic dysregulation and HIT pathogenesis[^2]. Third, a BJH study implementing a clinical decision support (CDS) tool using the HIT computerized risk (HIT-CR) score significantly increased diagnostic testing rates among patients with intermediate-to-high HIT probability (from 11.8% to 34.0%; p < 0.0001), demonstrating a practical translational strategy to reduce diagnostic under-ascertainment[^3]. Fourth, an AJH study of iron chelation in Diamond–Blackfan Anemia Syndrome (DBAS) patients aged under 3 years demonstrates that early deferasirox-based chelation initiated at a median serum ferritin of 1,340 ng/mL is feasible, effective (−11% per year reduction in ferritin; p < 0.001), and prevents cardiac iron overload — providing the first real-world evidence base for chelation management in very young transfusion-dependent infants[^4]. Fifth, a 10-year legacy review in RPTH marks the anniversary of the SIPPET trial, which originally established that plasma-derived FVIII products containing VWF carry lower inhibitor incidence than recombinant FVIII in previously untreated patients (PUPs) with severe hemophilia A — a finding with enduring guideline impact, now further contextualized by the rise of emicizumab[^5].

## II. TTP / HUS / Thrombotic Microangiopathy

A retrospective cohort study using the Clinical Practice Research Datalink (CPRD Aurum) linked to Hospital Episode Statistics characterised the clinical burden and healthcare resource utilisation of congenital TTP (cTTP) in England over the period 2000–2019[^7]. Among 36 identified patients with cTTP, matched 1:2 against non-TTP controls, the mean follow-up was 4.4 years in primary care and 5.7 years in secondary care. During follow-up, 38.9% of patients experienced at least one acute episode and 19.4% sustained an organ damage event. Healthcare resource utilisation was substantially elevated across all domains — inpatient admissions, outpatient appointments, and primary care contacts — relative to the non-TTP cohort. The average annual healthcare cost was £6,155 per cTTP patient compared with £858 for matched controls. Although limited by the rarity of the disease (n = 36), this study provides the most comprehensive English-language estimate of cTTP's economic footprint and underscores the unmet need for disease-modifying prevention strategies such as recombinant ADAMTS13, which remains in late-phase development.

A brief correspondence published in RPTH responds to an earlier letter on the epidemiology of immune-mediated TTP in Germany[^8], adding minor methodological commentary without new patient-level data. No new original clinical studies on caplacizumab, plasma exchange protocols, or iTTP biomarkers were identified this week.

## V. Hemophilia A & B (including gene therapy)

Two clinically relevant reports enrich the hemophilia literature this week. The first is a landmark 10-year retrospective from Peyvandi et al. reviewing the legacy of the SIPPET trial — the first randomised evidence that plasma-derived FVIII products (pdFVIII) containing von Willebrand factor are associated with a lower inhibitor incidence than recombinant FVIII (rFVIII) in previously untreated patients with severe hemophilia A[^5]. The RPTH commentary synthesises how post-SIPPET studies have since extended this evidence base, encompassing deeper insights into gene mutation-dependent immunogenicity, non-neutralising antibodies, IgG subclass heterogeneity, and epigenetic modulation of inhibitor development. Critically, the authors acknowledge that the increasing uptake of emicizumab has contributed to a measurable reduction in observed inhibitor incidence — though it remains unresolved whether this represents delayed FVIII exposure or a true reduction in immunogenicity. The authors argue that the mechanistic understanding of inhibitor formation during the early, immunologically vulnerable exposure window must remain a research priority even in the emicizumab era, given the global burden of inhibitor disease in resource-limited settings.

The second publication, from Spena et al. in RPTH, examined miR-128 expression in peripheral blood mononuclear cells and CD4+ naïve and memory T cells of 45 patients with severe hemophilia A stratified by inhibitor status (persistent n = 9, transient n = 10, no inhibitor n = 26)[^9]. A statistically significant upregulation of miR-128 was observed in PBMCs of patients with inhibitors (1.5-fold change; p = 0.027) and memory T cells (2.6-fold change; p = 0.015) compared with inhibitor-free patients. The effect was amplified in persistent inhibitor cases, with a 3.8-fold upregulation in memory T cells (p = 0.022). Given the known role of miR-128 in T cell differentiation and immune activation, these findings suggest that epigenetic microRNA-based dysregulation may contribute to the aberrant immunological milieu permissive to anti-FVIII antibody formation. The relatively small cohort, lack of prospective validation, and cross-sectional nature require cautious interpretation, but the study provides a candidate biomarker and therapeutic target warranting investigation in larger longitudinal cohorts of previously untreated patients.

## VI. Von Willebrand Disease

An analysis from O'Donnell et al. published in RPTH evaluates the extent to which menstrual and female-specific outcomes are incorporated into clinical interventional trials of VWD prophylaxis, drawing on searches of ClinicalTrials.gov, Health Canada, and EudraCT databases for studies open between 2014 and 2024[^10]. Of 42 studies initially identified, 10 met inclusion criteria after exclusions. Only 2 of 10 (20%) studies incorporated menstrual inclusion criteria, and 4 of 10 actively excluded treated menstrual bleeding from bleed eligibility criteria. Annualised bleed rates (ABR) — an endpoint adapted from haemophilia trial methodology — were collected in 80% of studies, but menstrual outcomes were captured in only 40%. Among 185 participants recruited across studies with posted results, identified females of menstrual age comprised only 65 (35%). These data expose a systematic sex-related evidence gap in VWD therapeutic trials, particularly given that heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) is the most frequently reported and most clinically impactful bleeding manifestation in females with VWD. The authors call for standardised, VWD-specific female-centred outcome measures and advocate for trial design reform to ensure that the prophylaxis evidence base reflects the predominant disease phenotype in this population.

## IX. Red Cell Disorders (G6PD, PK deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis, Diamond–Blackfan Anemia)

A French–Italian retrospective multicentre study by Torchio et al. examined iron chelation management in transfusion-dependent Diamond–Blackfan Anemia Syndrome (DBAS) patients who initiated chelation before the age of three years[^4]. Of 167 transfused DBAS patients from national registries, 64 (38%) began chelation at a median age of 18 months. Indications were serum ferritin ≥ 500 ng/mL (median at initiation: 1,340 ng/mL) and more than 10 prior transfusions. Deferasirox was used in 63% and deferoxamine in 35%. Chelation was associated with a statistically significant annual reduction in serum ferritin of −11% (p < 0.001). Among 28 patients with available ferritin at 5–6 years of age, 43% had levels below 500 ng/mL and none exceeded 2,000 ng/mL. Liver iron concentration, assessed in 31 patients at a median age of 3.2 years, showed severe overload in 45% at first evaluation. Importantly, among 22 patients who underwent cardiac MRI, no myocardial iron overload was detected at any time point, indicating that early chelation effectively prevents the most morbid complication of iron overload. These data represent the first real-world evidence on chelation in DBAS infants under 3 years of age, supporting the feasibility and cardiprotective value of early deferasirox-based therapy, with implications extending to other transfusion-dependent anemias of early childhood such as severe beta-thalassemia.

## X. Iron Deficiency & Nutritional Anemias

A mechanistic study published in Blood this week by Liu et al. explores the cooperative roles of transferrin receptor 1 (Tfr1) and transferrin receptor 2 (Tfr2) in hepatocellular iron sensing and hepcidin regulation[^11]. Using liver-specific double-knockout mice (TfrcAlb-Cre;Tfr2Alb-Cre), the authors demonstrate that Tfr1 plays a limited role in hepatocellular iron acquisition but negatively regulates hepcidin via interaction with the hemochromatosis protein Hfe, while Tfr2 is the primary positive regulator of hepcidin expression and an acute iron sensor. Under dietary iron restriction, hepcidin suppression was comparable in both Tfr2-deficient genotypes, but following an acute dietary iron challenge, Hamp mRNA induction and Smad1/5/9 phosphorylation occurred only in Tfr2-expressing livers — establishing that "liberated" Hfe requires Tfr2 co-operation to signal appropriately. The study refines the molecular hierarchy of iron-hepcidin signalling, with potential implications for the design of hepcidin-targeted therapies in iron-restrictive anaemias and hereditary hemochromatosis.

## XI. Thrombosis & Coagulation (VTE, APS, inherited thrombophilia)

Three clinically relevant contributions appear in the thrombosis/coagulation domain this week.

**HIT — Epigenetic pathogenesis.** A mechanistic Blood study by Yu et al. identifies JMJD1C — a member of the lysine-specific histone demethylase 3 subfamily — as a novel epigenetic regulator of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production and HIT pathogenesis[^2]. JMJD1C-deficient B cells demonstrated enhanced B cell receptor (BCR)-induced proliferation and transcriptomic upregulation of BCR signalling, NF-κB activation, cell-cycle, and SLE-associated pathways. CUT&Tag profiling showed that JMJD1C deficiency increased H3K36me1 modification at gene start sites in these pathways, driving epigenetic B cell hyperactivation. Crucially, transcriptional profiling and regulon analysis of B cells from HIT patients mirrored the JMJD1C-deficient phenotype closely, providing the first molecular link between epigenetic dysregulation and human HIT. This raises the theoretical possibility of future epigenetic modulation as a preventive or therapeutic strategy in HIT.

**HIT — Clinical decision support.** Complementing the mechanistic data above, Gallo et al. report a before-and-after implementation study of a clinical decision support (CDS) advisory system utilising the HIT-CR score in a large US healthcare system[^3]. The CDS was triggered by a HIT-CR score of 4 and provided temporal platelet count graphs with risk-stratification prompts. Among patients with 4Ts scores ≥ 4, diagnostic testing rates rose from 28/237 (11.8%) pre-intervention to 98/288 (34.0%) post-intervention (p < 0.0001). The proportion of patients determined to have HIT increased from 1.8% to 3.2%, though this difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.25). Fourteen of 26 patients with 4Ts scores ≥ 6 still did not undergo diagnostic testing despite the CDS advisory, and eight experienced possible complications attributable to undiagnosed HIT. These data confirm the value of automated clinical decision tools in reducing under-testing for HIT and suggest that more directive (rather than advisory) prompts may be needed for highest-risk patients.

**Cancer-associated VTE and inherited thrombophilia.** A Haematologica study by Stevens et al. leveraged UK Biobank data (n = 398,053, of whom 42,122 developed cancer) to evaluate seven thrombophilic variants (ABO, FGG, FXI, FVL, PTM, SLC44A2, and TSPAN15) in relation to cancer-associated thrombosis (CAT)[^12]. Factor V Leiden (FVL) conferred the highest relative risk of CAT (HR 1.81 for one risk allele; HR 5.75 for two risk alleles), followed by SLC44A2 (HR 1.51 for one risk allele; HR 1.53 for two risk alleles). For FVL heterozygotes, 2-year absolute VTE risk approximately doubled in several malignancies, rising from 5.17% to 9.26% in colorectal cancer and from 4.16% to 10.59% in lymphoma. Effects were greatest in high-risk cancers including pancreatic and lung tumours. These findings support the concept that germline thrombophilia profiling, particularly for FVL, may meaningfully improve personalised thromboprophylaxis decision-making in cancer patients, and are hypothesis-generating for incorporation into prospective CAT prevention trials alongside clinical risk scores such as the Khorana score.

**Coagulation activation in anorexia nervosa.** A cross-sectional case-control study by Egedal et al. in RPTH examined the coagulation profile of 40 female patients with anorexia nervosa (AN) compared with 40 matched healthy controls[^13]. Patients with AN had significantly elevated plasma tissue factor (TF) and prothrombin fragment 1+2 (F1+2), alongside higher C1 inhibitor and high-molecular-weight kininogen, with reduced kallikrein generation. Thrombin generation and ROTEM variables were not significantly different between groups. The authors interpret these findings as evidence of modest in vivo coagulation activation skewed toward the extrinsic pathway, with an imbalance rather than full procoagulant activation in the contact pathway. These results are hypothesis-generating and provide coagulation mechanistic context for the epidemiologically documented increased thrombosis risk in AN.

**DOAC reversal in urgent procedures.** A UK-wide audit by Murphy et al. in RPTH described real-world use of reversal agents prior to urgent procedures in 198 patients on DOACs[^14]. The median time from reversal to procedure was 1.6 hours; 76% of procedures occurred within 24 hours. Four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC) was used in 87% of cases, idarucizumab in 8%, and andexanet alfa in 5%. Tranexamic acid was administered in only 20%. Ninety-day mortality was 24%, and thromboembolic events occurred in 3% — all following 4F-PCC. The audit identifies opportunities to improve clinical workflows, including greater use of tranexamic acid, drug-level testing before reversal, and more rigorous risk stratification for low-risk procedures. These findings are of direct relevance to hematologists advising on perioperative anticoagulation management.

## XIII. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings from this week carry particular relevance for clinical practice at NCKUH and peer institutions in Taiwan. The final ANDROMEDA OS data for D-VCd in AL amyloidosis are immediately actionable: daratumumab (subcutaneous) is available in Taiwan under NHI coverage for AL amyloidosis following the approval of this indication, and clinicians should ensure that the 6-cycle VCd backbone plus single-agent daratumumab maintenance up to 24 total cycles is implemented per the ANDROMEDA protocol. The sustained hematologic CR rate of 59.5% and the organ response rates — cardiac and renal responses 2–3 times higher with D-VCd — should reinforce adherence to this standard.

For hemophilia management, Taiwan's NHI covers both plasma-derived FVIII/VWF products and recombinant FVIII concentrates, and emicizumab is reimbursed for severe hemophilia A patients with inhibitors. The SIPPET 10-year review is a timely reminder that product selection for previously untreated patients remains an immunogenically significant decision, particularly in resource-constrained contexts. Haematology centres at NTUH, VGH Taipei, and CMUH should review product allocation protocols for newly diagnosed severe hemophilia A infants in light of persistent SIPPET-era evidence.

Regarding iron chelation in young children, while DBAS is rare, Taiwan has a significant population of transfusion-dependent thalassemia major patients. The Torchio et al. data supporting early deferasirox initiation (median 18 months of age, ferritin ≥ 500 ng/mL) and its cardiac-protective efficacy provide a useful benchmark for paediatric haematologists managing very young thalassemia patients who require ongoing RBC transfusion support. Taiwan's national thalassemia screening programme enables early diagnosis; prompt linkage to early chelation pathways is achievable within the NHI framework.

For coagulation practice, the HIT-CDS implementation data (Gallo et al.) support adoption of algorithmic decision aids in Taiwanese academic centres — a practice potentially adaptable to the NCKUH electronic health record system to reduce under-diagnosis of HIT among patients on heparin infusions in haematology and ICU settings.

## XIV. Key Takeaways

The overarching themes of 2026-W20 in non-malignant hematology are as follows. First, the mature ANDROMEDA OS data definitively establish D-VCd as the only regimen proven to improve survival in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis, reinforcing universal implementation of this standard-of-care. Second, convergent mechanistic (JMJD1C/epigenetics) and translational (CDS implementation) data on HIT this week move the field simultaneously toward understanding immunological susceptibility and improving clinical recognition — two complementary objectives. Third, early chelation data in DBAS provide a proof-of-concept framework applicable to all very young transfusion-dependent infants, with the central message that cardiac iron overload is preventable with timely intervention. Fourth, the SIPPET 10-year retrospective and the miR-128 inhibitor study together underscore that immunogenicity in hemophilia A remains an active research frontier, particularly for the large number of PUPs in low- and middle-income countries not yet accessing emicizumab. Fifth, the VWD clinical trial landscape review exposes a structural gender equity gap in haemostasis research that requires urgently rectified trial design, with HMB deserving primary endpoint status rather than exclusion.
