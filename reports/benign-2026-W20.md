## Summary

The five most clinically actionable signals this week are as follows. First, the NXTAGE phase 1/2 trial of NXT007 — a next-generation FVIIIa-mimetic bispecific antibody administered subcutaneously every four weeks — reports annualised treated bleed rates of 0.00 in the two highest dose cohorts (0.70 mg/kg and 1.08 mg/kg), with predicted FVIII-equivalent activity reaching ≥40 IU/dL from cohort B2 onward, establishing proof of concept for a less-burdensome subcutaneous hemostatic agent in severe hemophilia A without inhibitors[^1]. Second, long-term follow-up data from the LTF-303 study demonstrate that betibeglogene autotemcel (beti-cel) achieves transfusion independence in 90.2% of phase 3 participants with transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia, sustained at a median of 5.9 years follow-up, with no malignancies or insertional oncogenesis reported[^2]. Third, a *Blood* study identifies JMJD1C — a lysine-specific histone demethylase — as a novel epigenetic regulator of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production in HIT, demonstrating that JMJD1C deficiency drives B-cell hyperactivation via H3K36me1 modification and NF-κB pathway upregulation, providing the first epigenetic mechanistic framework for HIT pathogenesis[^3]. Fourth, a *Blood Advances* study demonstrates that PROS1 intron 1 (~46 kb) contains discrete enhancer and repressor elements necessary for maintaining protein S expression in vivo, and that long-read sequencing identifies rare noncoding intron-1 variants in a patient with unexplained congenital protein S deficiency, supporting expansion of genetic testing beyond coding regions[^4]. Fifth, perioperative anticoagulation management data from 172 patients with thrombotic APS undergoing 282 planned procedures reveals a 30-day arterial thromboembolism risk of 0.7% (95% CI 0.2–2.6%) with guideline-directed care, with therapeutic-dose LMWH bridging used preferentially in triple-positive patients (OR 7.6; 95% CI 3.2–18.2)[^5].

## I. Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

The ITP literature this week contributes one new mechanistic study and an important safety signal, both distinct from findings reported in W19.

A preclinical study published in *Blood* from Yu et al. identifies JMJD1C, a member of the lysine-specific histone demethylase 3 subfamily, as an essential epigenetic regulator governing the production of pathogenic PF4/heparin antibodies — the hallmark of heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT)[^3]. While this work is primarily relevant to the HIT section below, the broader finding that JMJD1C deficiency promotes systemic autoimmune antibody production through enhanced BCR signaling and NF-κB pathway activation has mechanistic implications for all immune-mediated thrombocytopenias, including ITP. CUT&Tag profiling revealed increased H3K36me1 modification at gene start sites of BCR signaling, cell-cycle, and SLE-associated loci in JMJD1C-deficient B cells, mirroring transcriptional signatures found in B cells from HIT patients. Whether analogous epigenetic dysregulation operates in ITP-associated anti-platelet glycoprotein autoantibody-producing B cells warrants direct investigation.

A retrospective cohort study published in *RPTH* by Rajagopalan et al. prospectively characterizes thrombocytosis — the converse extreme of platelet count dysregulation — rather than thrombocytopenia per se, but provides clinically important context for neonatal/infant platelet biology[^6]. Analysis of 20,818 infants across two tertiary NICUs found thrombocytosis (>500 × 10³/μL) in 3% of all patients and 8% of preterm infants, substantially lower than the 20% rate at a quaternary NICU. Extreme thrombocytosis (>1,000 × 10³/μL) occurred in 0.08% versus 0.5% in the quaternary centre. Critically, thrombocytosis was associated with leukocytosis and relative anaemia but was not associated with thrombotic or bleeding complications. Vitamin D level was inversely correlated with platelet count among infants with thrombocytosis, while thyroid hormone and liver function tests were not — suggesting a specific Vitamin D–thrombocytosis axis rather than general organ immaturity. These data provide important reassurance against reflexive anticoagulation for extreme neonatal thrombocytosis in the absence of thrombotic events.

The W19 findings on sex-stratified proteomic/metabolomic biomarkers in paediatric ITP (ICAM-1, biopterin, ACTA-2, N6-acetyl-L-lysine)[^7] and HLA allele prognostication[^8], as well as the TPO-RA-associated myelofibrosis safety signal[^9], are not repeated here.

## II. TTP / HUS / Thrombotic Microangiopathy

A population-based health economics study from Barker et al. published in *BJH* provides the first linkage of primary and secondary care data to quantify the clinical and economic burden of congenital TTP (cTTP) in England[^10]. Using the Clinical Practice Research Datalink (Aurum) linked to Hospital Episode Statistics (2000–2019), 36 cTTP patients were identified and matched 1:2 to non-TTP controls. Mean follow-up was 4.4 and 5.7 years in primary and secondary care, respectively. Among the 36 cTTP patients, 38.9% experienced an acute episode and 19.4% an organ damage event during follow-up — rates disproportionate to this ultra-rare disease's prevalence. Healthcare resource utilization was markedly elevated: the average annual cost of care for a cTTP patient was £6,155, compared with £858 for matched controls — a 7.2-fold difference. These data underscore the case for disease-modifying prophylaxis (plasma infusion or recombinant ADAMTS13) in cTTP and establish a health-economic benchmark against which cost-effectiveness analyses of emerging prophylactic strategies can be measured.

No new data on immune-mediated TTP, caplacizumab, or complement-mediated HUS were identified this week beyond the methodological correspondence noted in W19[^11].

## III. Aplastic Anemia

A "How I Treat" review from Groarke, Gutierrez-Rodrigues, and Patel published in *Blood* provides a structured framework for utilizing somatic alterations in the diagnosis, risk stratification, and therapy of hypocellular bone marrow failure (BMF)[^12]. The authors synthesize how disease-specific clonal signatures — including PIGA mutations (suggestive of PNH/immune aplastic anemia), TP53 mutations (raising concern for hypoplastic MDS), and telomere biology gene mutations (pointing toward inherited BMF syndromes) — can aid etiological differentiation. For acquired immune aplastic anemia (IAA), the presence of certain clonal alterations (e.g., BCOR/BCORL1, DNMT3A) informs risk of secondary myeloid neoplasm and may guide surveillance intensity. The review emphasizes that the clinical significance of clonal hematopoiesis in BMF differs substantially from that in the general population and argues for integration of somatic mutation profiling into standard diagnostic algorithms rather than as an ancillary test. While this is a narrative "How I Treat" without primary outcome data, it reflects evolving NHLBI/NIH consensus practice and is directly relevant to clinical decision-making at institutions managing IAA and IBMFS.

A brief editorial in *Haematologica* by Bierings and Thus, titled "Immune mediated marrow failure in very young children: horse ATG once again out of the magician's hat"[^13], highlights renewed interest in horse ATG (hATG) for very young children with immune-mediated BMF, in whom allogeneic HSCT carries greater procedural risk. No quantitative data were available in the abstract at time of compilation.

## V. Hemophilia A & B (including gene therapy)

This week yields two landmark publications in hemophilia A that are among the most clinically significant findings of 2026-W20.

Nogami et al. report the primary analysis of the multiple-ascending-dose Part B of the NXTAGE phase 1/2 trial of NXT007, a next-generation activated FVIII-mimetic bispecific antibody, in men with severe hemophilia A without FVIII inhibitors[^1]. Four dose cohorts were studied: B1 (0.072 mg/kg, n=10), B2 (0.28 mg/kg, n=6), B3 (0.70 mg/kg, n=6), and B4 (1.08 mg/kg, n=8), all administered subcutaneously every four weeks. Median treatment durations were 114.1, 96.4, 58.1, and 22.2 weeks across cohorts B1–B4, respectively. Plasma NXT007 concentration increased dose-dependently, with predicted FVIII-equivalent activity reaching nonhemophilic levels (≥40 IU/dL) in cohort B2 and above. Mean annualised treated bleed rates were 1.48, 0.28, 0.00, and 0.00 for cohorts B1 through B4, respectively — demonstrating complete bleed prevention at the two highest doses. The safety profile was favorable across all doses; most adverse events were mild to moderate, all three serious adverse events were considered unrelated to NXT007, and two participants developed pharmacokinetics-affecting anti-drug antibodies, of whom one discontinued (cohort B1). Lillicrap's accompanying editorial contextualizes NXT007 within the "generational advancement" of FVIIIa mimetics, positioning this subcutaneous once-monthly regimen as potentially offering advantages over emicizumab in terms of coagulation activity ceiling[^14]. NXTAGE Part B establishes NXT007 as a compelling candidate for phase 3 investigation, particularly relevant for patients who require higher FVIII-equivalent activity than emicizumab currently provides.

A parallel preclinical study from Chou et al. in *Blood Advances* reports the development of a novel humanized hemophilia A mouse model (HemoAhfIX/hfX) via AAV delivery of human F9 and F10, expressing human FIX at 158.0 ± 57.9 IU/dL and FX at 372.8 ± 170.7 IU/dL at 12 weeks[^15]. Using a 2-factor factorial design in 55 mice assigned across four treatment arms (FVIII prophylaxis, on-demand FVIII, emicizumab + FVIII prophylaxis, emicizumab + on-demand FVIII), the study demonstrated that emicizumab treatment was associated with significantly attenuated inhibitor titers across treatment groups (p = 0.0006) and preserved joint architecture against bleeding challenge. Although inhibitor development rates could not be distinguished statistically across groups due to high overall inhibitor incidence, these findings establish this model as a platform for evaluating FVIII inhibitor risk under emicizumab prophylaxis — a critical unresolved clinical question. This model, notably developed by a Taiwan-affiliated group (NTU-affiliated investigators), has direct relevance to PUP management decisions.

## VII. Thalassemia (alpha & beta; Taiwan-relevant)

Long-term follow-up data from the LTF-303 study of betibeglogene autotemcel (beti-cel) for transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia (TDT), published in *Blood* by Kwiatkowski et al., represent the most mature gene therapy efficacy and safety dataset for this disease to date[^2]. Sixty-three participants (median age 17 years, range 4–35) who received beti-cel in phase 1/2 (n=22) or phase 3 (n=41) studies were enrolled in LTF-303 with a median follow-up of 5.9 years (range 2.9–10.1). Transfusion independence (TI) was achieved by 15 of 22 (68.2%) phase 1/2 participants and 37 of 41 (90.2%) phase 3 participants — the improvement attributable to manufacturing refinements that increased transduction efficiency, drug product vector copy number, and HbAT87Q levels. Among those achieving TI, median weighted average hemoglobin was 10.2 g/dL (phase 1/2) and 11.2 g/dL (phase 3). TI was sustained through last follow-up across both cohorts. Among participants achieving TI, 38 of 52 (73%) had discontinued iron chelation at last follow-up, with no increase in liver iron concentration, and markers of ineffective erythropoiesis (serum transferrin receptor, erythropoietin) improved with restoration of iron homeostasis. Health-related quality-of-life scores showed durable improvements. Critically, no malignancies, insertional oncogenesis, or replication-competent lentivirus were detected across the entire cohort over up to 10.1 years — addressing the primary long-term safety concern with lentiviral vector gene therapy. An accompanying perspective by Frangoul in *Blood* asks whether gene therapy constitutes a cure for β-thalassemia[^16], contextualizing these data within the broader landscape including CRISPR-based approaches (e.g., exagamglogene autotemcel/exa-cel).

## X. Iron Deficiency & Nutritional Anemias

A mechanistically important study from Liu et al. published in *Blood* dissects the cooperative and non-redundant roles of transferrin receptor 1 (Tfr1) and transferrin receptor 2 (Tfr2) in hepatic iron sensing and hepcidin regulation[^17]. Using hepatocyte-specific dual knockout mice (TfrcAlb-Cre;Tfr2Alb-Cre), the authors demonstrate that while both receptors are dispensable for hepatocellular iron acquisition, Tfr2 and HFE exhibit non-redundant functions under chronic iron loading but act cooperatively to induce hepcidin in response to an acute iron challenge. Specifically, following an acute dietary iron challenge, Hamp mRNA induction and Smad1,5,9 phosphorylation occurred only in Tfr2-expressing livers, indicating that "liberated" HFE (released from Tfr1 upon iron loading) requires Tfr2 to become functionally active. Under chronic iron restriction, Hamp mRNA suppression was equivalent in Tfr2-deficient livers regardless of compensatory Tfr1 upregulation in the control. These findings refine the molecular model of iron-sensing at the hepatocyte, with direct implications for understanding the spectrum of hereditary hemochromatosis phenotypes and for the rational design of hepcidin-modulating therapies. Distinguishing the Tfr1–HFE–Tfr2 triad's behaviour under acute versus chronic iron loading is particularly relevant for clinical scenarios involving rapid iron flux, such as post-transfusion iron overload in thalassemia or MDS.

An observational study from Tobiasch et al. in *Blood Advances* characterises functional iron deficiency induced by a single extreme endurance event (86.9 km World Mountain & Trail Running Championships, +6,500 m ascent)[^18]. Serum and peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) analyses from seven elite athletes before and after the event demonstrated an IL-6–driven rise in hepcidin and ferritin, with concurrent declines in serum iron and transferrin saturation consistent with inflammation-driven functional iron deficiency. Biochemical signs of intravascular hemolysis were also evident. At the cellular level, PBMCs displayed upregulation of hypoxia-inducible and iron uptake genes and downregulation of iron storage/export genes. While this is a small physiological study without clinical intervention, it mechanistically confirms the hepcidin–IL-6 axis as the dominant driver of exercise-induced iron restriction and has implications for diagnosing iron deficiency in high-performance athletes — a population presenting increasingly in haematology clinics.

## XI. Thrombosis & Coagulation (VTE, APS, inherited thrombophilia)

This section contains the most concentrated cluster of new high-quality data in W20.

**Antiphospholipid Syndrome — Perioperative Management.** Paquette et al. report the first dedicated perioperative anticoagulation cohort study in thrombotic APS, encompassing 172 patients who underwent 282 planned periprocedural anticoagulation interruptions between 2016 and 2025[^5]. Most patients (84.9%) received warfarin, and 25% were triple-positive (anticardiolipin, anti-β2-glycoprotein I, and lupus anticoagulant). LMWH bridging was used in 84.7% of warfarin interruptions; therapeutic-dose bridging was more frequently applied in triple-positive patients (OR 7.6; 95% CI 3.2–18.2) and those with prior arterial thromboembolism (ATE; OR 3.4; 95% CI 1.5–7.8). The 30-day risk of ATE was 0.7% (95% CI 0.2–2.6%), major bleeding occurred in 0.7% (95% CI 0.2–2.6%; one fatal event, mortality risk 0.4%; 95% CI 0.1–2.0%), and clinically relevant non-major bleeding in 3.2% (95% CI 1.7–6.0%). A single case of probable catastrophic APS occurred in a triple-positive patient receiving only postoperative prophylactic-dose LMWH, reinforcing that triple-positive patients require therapeutic, not prophylactic, bridging. These findings differ from and extend the W19 APS recurrence data (which addressed long-term thrombotic and bleeding risk on indefinite anticoagulation[^19]) by providing the first perioperative risk estimates.

**Protein S Deficiency — Noncoding Variant Discovery.** Maruyama et al. demonstrate in *Blood Advances* that a ~46-kb first intron of PROS1 contains regulatory elements essential for protein S (PS) expression across tissues[^4]. Reporter assays in HepG2 cells identified discrete intron-1 regions with enhancer and repressor activity; CRISPR/Cas9 deletion of intron 1 reduced endogenous PS mRNA and secreted PS protein. Mice with large intron-1 deletions showed markedly reduced PS antigen in plasma and platelets versus wild type, with tissue-dependent variability across organs. Most clinically translatable, long-read sequencing of a patient with congenital PS deficiency who was coding-region negative by conventional testing identified rare intron-1 variants that reduced reporter activity. These data provide the first direct evidence that intronic regulatory variants contribute to congenital PS deficiency in humans, with immediate implications for the genetic diagnostic workup of unexplained inherited thrombophilia.

**Cancer-Associated VTE and Thrombophilic Variants.** Stevens et al. examined seven prothrombotic genetic variants in 398,053 UK Biobank participants, of whom 42,122 developed cancer[^20]. In the non-cancer population, all seven variants (ABO, FGG, FXI, FVL, PTM, SLC44A2, TSPAN15) were associated with elevated VTE risk. In cancer patients, Factor V Leiden (FVL) conferred the highest risk (HR 1.81 for one risk allele; HR 5.75 for two risk alleles), followed by SLC44A2 (HR 1.51 for one risk allele, 1.53 for two risk alleles). Genetic effects were largest in high-risk cancers including pancreatic and lung malignancies. For FVL heterozygotes, 2-year absolute VTE risk approximately doubled in several tumour types: from 5.17% to 9.26% in colorectal cancer and from 4.16% to 10.59% in lymphoma. The Aalen–Johansen method was used for absolute risk estimation treating death as a competing event. These findings are directly relevant to personalized thromboprophylaxis decision-making in haematology-oncology patients and suggest that genetic thrombophilia testing could be integrated with clinical risk scores (e.g., Khorana score) to improve CAT prophylaxis targeting.

**Superficial Vein Thrombosis.** Oblitas et al. published in *BJH* a comparative study of thromboembolic risk in superficial vein thrombosis involving the saphenopopliteal versus saphenofemoral junction[^21]. No quantitative abstract data were available at time of compilation; the full article should be consulted when accessible.

**IL-4 as a Physiological Antiplatelet Mediator.** Lin et al. demonstrate in *Blood* that interleukin-4 (IL-4), an anti-inflammatory cytokine, directly inhibits collagen-induced platelet activation by binding to residues 38–46 within the extracellular collagen-binding domain of glycoprotein VI (GPVI), suppressing phospholipase Cγ2 and protein kinase C phosphorylation[^22]. In vivo, IL-4 overexpression or intravenous administration prolonged bleeding time and attenuated both arterial and venous thrombosis in murine models. Conversely, IL-4 knockout or anti-IL-4 antibody treatment enhanced platelet reactivity and promoted thrombosis. A GPVI-derived interfering peptide (RR9) that disrupts the IL-4–GPVI complex reversed these inhibitory effects. This discovery establishes a direct mechanistic link between systemic anti-inflammatory cytokine status and platelet thrombotic function, potentially explaining the paradoxically elevated thrombotic risk in inflammatory states where IL-4 is consumed or neutralized. Whether IL-4 levels predict antiplatelet efficacy or thrombotic risk in clinical APS or inflammatory conditions warrants prospective evaluation.

**PAI-1 siRNA for Thrombosis Prevention.** Ferraresso et al. published in *Blood* a preclinical siRNA-lipid nanoparticle approach (siPAI-1) that achieved 90% knockdown of plasma PAI-1 lasting 10 days after a single dose in mice, without overt toxicity[^23]. In young and aged mice, siPAI-1 decreased thrombus weight after complete inferior vena cava ligation and increased survival in aged mice at 4 days post-ligation. In diet-induced obese mice, hepatic PAI-1 mRNA was >10-fold elevated compared to healthy controls; siPAI-1 achieved 70% knockdown of circulating PAI-1. The study also demonstrated lifespan prolongation in a fast-aging mouse model. While early-stage, these findings position PAI-1 siRNA as a potential long-acting antithrombotic strategy, particularly for age- and obesity-related thrombosis, with a mechanism complementary to direct anticoagulants.

## XIII. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings reported this week carry particular relevance for clinical practice in Taiwan. The beti-cel long-term data (Section VII) are directly applicable to Taiwan, where β-thalassemia affects approximately 1–2% of the population as carriers and transfusion-dependent major disease is managed at NTUH, CGMH, and CMUH thalassemia centres. Taiwan's NHI does not currently reimburse beti-cel or exa-cel; however, the 10-year safety data confirming absence of insertional oncogenesis substantially strengthens the evidence base for future reimbursement applications. NCKUH's thalassemia programme may consider early access through expanded clinical trials as Phase 3 results consolidate.

The emicizumab inhibitor-risk model developed by the NTU-affiliated group (Chou et al.)[^15] is noteworthy as Taiwanese-origin preclinical work that directly informs clinical guidance on FVIII exposure in emicizumab-treated patients undergoing surgery or managing acute bleeds — a practical question encountered regularly at hemophilia treatment centres including NCKUH. Emicizumab is currently reimbursed by NHI for hemophilia A patients with inhibitors; the question of prophylaxis in PUPs without inhibitors is under discussion nationally.

Regarding inherited thrombophilia, the cancer-associated VTE data linking FVL to doubled 2-year VTE absolute risk in lymphoma patients[^20] require recontextualization for Taiwanese patients: the FVL (F5 c.1691G>A) and prothrombin G20210A mutations have very low prevalence in East Asian populations (<0.1%), and the SLC44A2 variant frequency also differs from European populations. Clinicians at NCKUH should therefore not directly apply absolute risk estimates from UK Biobank to Taiwanese oncology patients; Asian-specific thrombophilia panels prioritising AT deficiency, protein C/S deficiency, and the PROC/PROS1 genetic landscape are more informative in this setting. The noncoding PROS1 intron-1 data[^4] may be particularly relevant given the higher prevalence of protein S deficiency in East Asia.

The perioperative APS management study[^5] is immediately practice-relevant: APS is encountered across rheumatology-haematology joint clinics at NCKUH and other academic centres, and the quantification of 30-day ATE risk (0.7%) and catastrophic APS risk in triple-positive patients undergoing prophylactic-only bridging provides actionable decision support for the perioperative team.

## XIV. Key Takeaways

Five practice-changing or hypothesis-generating findings this week warrant direct attention for the NCKUH haematology team. NXT007 demonstrates zero bleeds in high-dose cohorts with subcutaneous once-monthly dosing, establishing a plausible challenger to emicizumab and representing the most advanced next-generation FVIIIa mimetic in the pipeline; a phase 3 programme should be followed closely. The LTF-303 beti-cel data now provide the longest gene therapy follow-up for TDT, confirming durable transfusion independence and freedom from genotoxicity at nearly 10 years, substantially de-risking the therapeutic approach. The JMJD1C epigenetic pathway in HIT is a conceptually novel finding that reframes HIT as not merely an immune activation disorder but one with identifiable epigenetic predisposition, opening therapeutic and diagnostic avenues. The identification of PROS1 intron-1 regulatory variants as a cause of congenital protein S deficiency should prompt haematology laboratories to adopt long-read sequencing as part of the thrombophilia workup in coding-region-negative cases. Finally, the perioperative APS data provide the first empirical risk estimates to guide bridging decisions in clinical practice, supporting a policy of therapeutic LMWH in all triple-positive APS patients undergoing invasive procedures.
