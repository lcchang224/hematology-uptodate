## Summary

The five most clinically actionable signals this week are as follows. First, the ANDROMEDA trial final survival analysis (median follow-up 61.4 months) confirms that daratumumab-VCd (D-VCd) achieves an updated hematologic complete response rate of 59.5% versus 19.2% for VCd alone (OR 6.03; 95% CI 3.80–9.58; p < 0.0001), with significant overall survival benefit (HR 0.62; 95% CI 0.42–0.90; p = 0.0121) in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis — the first randomized OS advantage in this disease[^1]. Second, a *Blood* study using CUT&Tag and RNA-Seq profiling establishes JMJD1C histone demethylase deficiency as a novel epigenetic driver of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production in HIT, revealing a mechanistic link between H3K36me1 dysregulation and pathogenic B-cell hyperactivation[^2]. Third, a matched sibling donor HSCT series of 85 paediatric and young adult SCD patients using the APOLLO protocol (post-transplant cyclophosphamide-based GVHD prophylaxis) demonstrates estimated event-free survival of 94.11% and overall survival of 96.47% at a median follow-up of 1191 days, with age >10 years being the only independent adverse factor (OS 100% vs 89.28%, p = 0.007)[^3]. Fourth, a real-world UK multicentre analysis of 198 patients receiving DOAC reversal agents prior to urgent procedures reveals that 4F-PCC was used in 87% of cases — mostly off-label for factor Xa inhibitors — with a 90-day mortality of 24% and thromboembolism in 3%, underscoring the absence of a unified evidence base for this common practice[^4]. Fifth, a 10-year legacy review of the SIPPET trial, published in *RPTH*, synthesizes evidence that plasma-derived FVIII products containing VWF confer lower inhibitor incidence than recombinant FVIII in previously untreated patients with severe hemophilia A, and explores epigenetic and immunologic mechanisms underlying this difference[^5].

## I. Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

The previously reported concern regarding TPO-RA overuse and myelofibrosis risk in paediatric ITP[^6] (noted in W19) had no abstract-level quantitative data available at that time; the full publication in *Haematologica* by Ma et al. remains without a publicly available detailed abstract as of this report compilation, and thus no new quantitative signal can be extracted this week.

A distinct and novel mechanistic observation with direct relevance to ITP-adjacent immune thrombocytopenic disorders comes from a *Blood* study by Yu et al. identifying JMJD1C, a lysine-specific histone demethylase 3 subfamily member, as an essential epigenetic regulator of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production and, more broadly, of B-cell immune tolerance[^2]. Although this study is primarily framed in the context of HIT (discussed further in Section II), the upstream findings are conceptually relevant to ITP: JMJD1C-deficient B cells were hyperresponsive, exhibiting enhanced BCR-induced proliferation and upregulation of BCR signaling, NF-κB activation, cell cycle, and SLE-associated pathways. CUT&Tag profiling revealed increased H3K36me1 modification at gene start sites in these pathways. Transcriptional profiling of B cells from HIT patients mirrored JMJD1C-deficient B cells, establishing a proof-of-concept that epigenetic dysregulation can drive autoantibody-mediated thrombocytopenia. Whether analogous JMJD1C alterations operate in classic ITP remains to be investigated.

A clinical decision support (CDS) study in *BJH* by Gallo et al. evaluated implementation of the HIT-computerized risk (HIT-CR) score in a large healthcare system[^7]. Although primarily a HIT diagnostic tool study, the findings demonstrate that an automated platelet count trajectory surveillance system with CDS alerts significantly improved the rate of diagnostic testing in patients at intermediate-to-high risk (4Ts ≥4): testing rates increased from 11.8% (28/237) pre-intervention to 34.0% (98/288) post-intervention (p < 0.0001). This infrastructure has potential applicability to thrombocytopenic patients more broadly, including ITP, where electronic monitoring of platelet kinetics could expedite diagnosis.

## II. TTP / HUS / Thrombotic Microangiopathy

A *BJH* study by Barker et al. provides the first population-level characterization of clinical burden and healthcare resource utilization for congenital TTP (cTTP) in England, using linked CPRD Aurum and Hospital Episode Statistics data from 2000–2019[^8]. Thirty-six patients with cTTP were identified and matched 1:2 to non-TTP controls. Among the 36 patients, 38.9% experienced an acute episode and 19.4% experienced an organ damage event during follow-up (mean 4.4 years in primary care, 5.7 years in secondary care). Healthcare resource utilization was substantially higher in cTTP patients relative to controls, with an average annual healthcare cost of £6,155 versus £858 in the non-TTP cohort — a 7.2-fold cost differential. These data quantify the economic and clinical burden of cTTP in the UK context and provide an evidence base for health technology assessments of novel prophylactic strategies including recombinant ADAMTS13.

A case series published in *AJH* by Scheen et al. describes thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA), cryoglobulinemia, and acute kidney injury in patients with monoclonal gammopathy[^9]. No quantitative abstract was available at time of compilation; the full manuscript should be reviewed for classification of TMA subtypes and complement-versus-non-complement-mediated pathways in this clinical context.

A correspondence in *RPTH* (Scharenberg et al.) provides a reply regarding the epidemiology of immune TTP in Germany[^10], but no new primary data are presented. The exchange underscores ongoing heterogeneity in iTTP incidence estimates across European registries and the need for harmonized diagnostic criteria.

## V. Hemophilia A & B (including gene therapy)

Two publications this week significantly advance the understanding of immunogenicity in severe hemophilia A. A landmark 10-year retrospective review of the SIPPET trial by Peyvandi et al., published in *RPTH*, synthesizes the durable impact of the first randomized evidence that plasma-derived FVIII (pdFVIII) containing von Willebrand factor is associated with a lower inhibitor incidence than recombinant FVIII (rFVIII) in previously untreated patients (PUPs)[^5]. The review contextualizes post-SIPPET mechanistic work, including insights into the immunogenicity of FVIII products as influenced by gene mutations, non-neutralizing antibodies, IgG subclass responses, epitope specificity, and epigenetic modulation. Crucially, the authors note that the advent of non-replacement therapies such as emicizumab has contributed to a marked observed decline in FVIII inhibitor incidence in recent years, but question whether this decline reflects truly reduced immunogenicity or simply delayed FVIII exposure in an era of bypassing-agent prophylaxis. This distinction has profound implications for future inhibitor epidemiology and for NHI coverage decisions regarding factor product selection in PUPs.

A complementary mechanistic study by Spena et al. in *RPTH* investigated miR-128 expression in peripheral blood mononuclear cells and CD4+ naïve and memory T cells from 45 patients with severe hemophilia A stratified by inhibitor status[^12]. A statistically significant 1.5-fold upregulation of miR-128 was observed in PBMCs (p = 0.027) and a 2.6-fold upregulation in memory T cells (p = 0.015) of patients with inhibitors (persistent or transient) compared with non-inhibitor patients. Among patients with persistent inhibitors specifically, memory T cell miR-128 expression was 3.8-fold higher than in non-inhibitor patients (p = 0.022). These findings implicate miR-128-mediated regulation of T-cell differentiation genes as a contributing mechanism to FVIII immunogenicity, providing a potential epigenetic biomarker for inhibitor risk stratification in clinical practice.

A *RPTH* review by O'Donnell et al. critically evaluates the inclusion of menstrual and female-specific outcomes in 10 eligible interventional VWD prophylaxis trials from 2014–2024[^13]. Only 2 of 10 studies (20%) incorporated menstrual inclusion criteria, while 4 of 10 specifically excluded treated menstrual bleeding from bleed eligibility criteria. Annualized bleed rates (ABRs) — metrics adapted from hemophilia trials — were collected in 80% of studies, whereas menstrual outcomes were reported in only 40%. Females of menstrual age constituted only 35% (65/185) of identified enrolled participants. This systematic gap highlights an unmet need for female-tailored trial designs in VWD and has direct implications for guideline development on VWD prophylaxis for heavy menstrual bleeding (see also Section VI).

## VI. Von Willebrand Disease

As detailed in Section V, the *RPTH* review by O'Donnell et al. documents the systematic under-representation of menstrual outcomes in VWD prophylaxis trials[^13]. Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) remains the most frequently reported and severe bleeding symptom in females with VWD, yet the field continues to rely on ABR frameworks derived from hemophilia studies. The review identifies that most studies with results posted reported age and sex but lacked overlapping data to identify females of menstrual age, limiting meaningful sub-analyses. This methodological critique is directly actionable: future study designs for VWD prophylaxis agents — including recombinant VWF, extended half-life products, and fitusiran — should prospectively incorporate pictorial blood assessment charts (PBAC), iron indices, and quality-of-life instruments specific to menstrual bleeding.

## IX. Red Cell Disorders (G6PD, PK deficiency, hereditary spherocytosis; Diamond-Blackfan Anemia)

A French–Italian multicenter retrospective registry study by Torchio et al., published in *AJH*, provides the first dataset specifically addressing iron chelation in Diamond-Blackfan Anemia Syndrome (DBAS) patients under 3 years of age[^14]. Among 167 transfused DBAS patients screened, 64 (38%) initiated chelation before age three (median age: 18 months). Indications were serum ferritin ≥500 ng/mL (median at initiation: 1,340 ng/mL) after more than 10 transfusions. Deferasirox was used in 63% of patients and deferoxamine in 35%. Chelation was associated with a significant reduction in serum ferritin levels (−11% per year; p < 0.001). At follow-up ages 5–6 years, ferritin data were available in 28 patients: 43% had levels below 500 ng/mL, and none exceeded 2,000 ng/mL. Liver iron concentration assessed in 31 of 64 patients (48%) at a median age of 3.2 years showed severe overload in 45% at first evaluation. Importantly, among 22 patients who underwent cardiac magnetic resonance imaging, no myocardial iron overload was detected — suggesting that early chelation initiated at current threshold criteria is sufficient to protect the heart in very young patients. These data provide the first evidence base to guide chelation decisions in infants and toddlers with transfusion-dependent DBAS, and by extension may inform similar strategies in other transfusion-dependent anemias including beta-thalassemia major.

## X. Iron Deficiency & Nutritional Anemias

A *Blood* study by Liu et al. provides important mechanistic clarity on the roles of transferrin receptor 1 (Tfr1) and transferrin receptor 2 (Tfr2) in hepatocellular iron sensing and hepcidin regulation[^15]. Using mice with hepatocyte-specific dual ablation of both Tfr1 and Tfr2, the authors demonstrate that transferrin receptors are largely dispensable for hepatocellular iron supply. Critically, Tfr2 and Hfe exhibit non-redundant functions under chronic iron loading but act cooperatively to induce hepcidin in response to an acute iron challenge: following a dietary iron bolus, Hamp mRNA induction and Smad1/5/9 phosphorylation occurred only in Tfr2-expressing livers, indicating that "liberated" Hfe (freed from Tfr1 competition) requires Tfr2 to become functionally active. This mechanistic resolution has direct implications for the molecular classification of hemochromatosis subtypes (HFE-related type 1 versus Tfr2-related type 3) and for designing hepcidin-modulating therapies in iron-restrictive anemias. The finding that acute hepcidin induction absolutely requires Tfr2 suggests that Tfr2 agonism or stabilization could represent a therapeutic strategy in conditions characterized by pathologically suppressed hepcidin, such as thalassemia-associated iron overload.

## XI. Thrombosis & Coagulation (VTE, APS, inherited thrombophilia)

**Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia — Epigenetic Mechanism.** Yu et al., publishing in *Blood*, report the first evidence linking epigenetic regulation to HIT pathogenesis[^2]. JMJD1C, a histone H3K9 demethylase, was found to be an essential regulator of PF4/heparin-specific antibody production. JMJD1C-deficient mice displayed disrupted immune tolerance with production of self-reactive antibodies including PF4/heparin-reactive platelet-activating antibodies. Mechanistically, JMJD1C deficiency leads to increased H3K36me1 at BCR signaling, NF-κB, cell cycle, and SLE-associated pathway gene start sites, driving B-cell hyperactivation. Crucially, transcriptional profiling of B cells from human HIT patients closely mirrored the JMJD1C-deficient B-cell signature, validating the clinical relevance of this epigenetic axis. These findings open a new paradigm for understanding why certain patients develop pathogenic HIT antibodies while others do not, and may ultimately inform risk-stratification tools to identify heparin-treated patients at highest immunological risk.

**HIT Diagnostic Testing — Clinical Decision Support.** The *BJH* study by Gallo et al. demonstrates that deployment of an automated CDS tool based on the HIT-CR score in a large health system significantly improved diagnostic testing rates from 11.8% to 34.0% among patients with 4Ts score ≥4 (p < 0.0001)[^7]. However, 14 of 26 patients with 4Ts ≥6 still did not undergo recommended diagnostic testing despite CDS alerts, and 8 experienced possible complications from undiagnosed HIT. This study confirms both the utility and limitations of algorithmic HIT surveillance and reinforces that optimized CDS design — including hard stops or mandatory acknowledgment for the highest-risk patients — may be required to capture the most vulnerable individuals.

**DOAC Reversal Agents in Urgent Procedures.** Murphy et al., publishing in *RPTH*, present a UK-wide HaemSTAR network audit of 198 patients receiving DOAC reversal agents prior to urgent procedures between October 2020 and June 2023[^4]. Procedures were rated as high-risk bleeding risk in 48% of cases. The median time from reversal to procedure was 1.6 hours and 76% of procedures occurred within 24 hours of reversal. Four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC) was used in 87% of patients, idarucizumab in 8%, and andexanet alfa in only 5%. Tranexamic acid was co-administered in only 20% of cases. Ninety-day mortality was 24%, and thromboembolic events occurred in 3% — all following 4F-PCC use. Drug-level testing prior to reversal was performed infrequently, and several procedures were classified as low-risk, raising questions about appropriate indications. These data highlight the dominance of off-label 4F-PCC use in UK practice despite the availability of andexanet alfa for anti-Xa reversal, and underscore opportunities to standardize reversal pathways through prospective trials.

**Coagulation Activation in Anorexia Nervosa.** A Danish cross-sectional case-control study by Egedal et al. in *RPTH* compared coagulation profiles between 40 female patients with anorexia nervosa (AN) and 40 age-matched controls[^16]. Patients with AN had significantly higher plasma concentrations of tissue factor (TF), prothrombin fragment 1+2 (F1+2), C1 inhibitor, and high-molecular-weight kininogen, alongside reduced kallikrein generation — consistent with modest in vivo coagulation activation via the extrinsic pathway. No group differences were found in thrombin generation or rotational thromboelastometry (ROTEM) variables, suggesting the overall hemostatic balance is maintained but with a procoagulant shift at a subclinical level. The authors appropriately characterize these findings as hypothesis-generating, requiring replication in larger prospective cohorts before guiding clinical thromboprophylaxis decisions in AN patients.

**Infant Thrombocytosis — Clinical Guidance.** Rajagopalan et al. report a retrospective analysis of 20,818 infants hospitalized in two tertiary NICUs (2011–2023), finding thrombocytosis (platelet count >500 × 10³/μL) in 3% of all patients and 8% of preterm infants — substantially lower than the 20% rate observed in a quaternary NICU comparator cohort[^17]. Extreme thrombocytosis (>1,000 × 10³/μL) occurred in 0.08% versus 0.5% at the quaternary unit. Thrombocytosis was associated with leukocytosis and relative anemia, but importantly was not associated with thrombotic or bleeding complications. Vitamin D level (inversely correlated with platelet count), rather than thyroid hormone or liver function, emerged as a novel associative factor among infants with thrombocytosis. These findings support a conservative approach to evaluation of neonatal thrombocytosis in tertiary care settings and argue against routine invasive workup or anticoagulation in the absence of complications.

**Red Cell Alloimmunization — Risk Factors.** Wang et al. in *BJH* conducted a case-control study using the REDS-III BioLINCC dataset, including 19,697 controls and 861 cases of RBC alloimmunization (anti-E, -K, -Jka, -C, and -c)[^18]. In multivariable analysis, myelodysplastic syndrome was associated with the highest number of alloantibody specificities, followed by sickle cell disease, rheumatoid arthritis, and lupus. ABO non-identical donor-recipient pairs were associated with higher odds of alloimmunization for all specificities evaluated. RBC storage duration showed significance only for anti-E after multivariable adjustment, with units stored >12 days associated with reduced odds of anti-E formation. These data have direct relevance to transfusion practice in hematology wards, where patients with SCD and MDS are disproportionately alloimmunized and may benefit from extended phenotype-matched transfusion protocols.

**Prothrombotic Variants and Cancer-Associated VTE.** Stevens et al., publishing in *Haematologica*, evaluated seven thrombophilic variants (ABO, FGG, FXI, FVL, PTM, SLC44A2, TSPAN15) in 42,122 cancer patients from the UK Biobank[^19]. CAT incidence was 7.82% in cancer patients versus 2.08% in non-cancer participants. Factor V Leiden (FVL) conferred the highest cancer-associated VTE risk (HR 1.81 for one risk allele; HR 5.75 for two risk alleles), followed by SLC44A2 (HR 1.51 for one allele; 1.53 for two alleles). Genetic effects were greatest in high-risk cancers. For FVL heterozygotes, 2-year absolute VTE risk approximately doubled in several malignancies: from 5.17% to 9.26% in colorectal cancer and from 4.16% to 10.59% in lymphoma. Although this study is primarily oncology-focused, the findings are immediately actionable in hematology practice: lymphoma patients with FVL heterozygosity carry a clinically significant 2.5-fold absolute VTE risk increment that may warrant earlier initiation of thromboprophylaxis and should be factored into Khorana score-based decisions.

## XIII. Taiwan Clinical Context

Several findings in this week's report have direct relevance to clinical practice at NCKUH and partner institutions. The ANDROMEDA final OS analysis[^1] confirms D-VCd as the evidence-based standard of care for newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis; NCKUH hematology-cardiology collaborative clinics managing suspected amyloidosis should apply the updated BSH early diagnosis guideline[^20] and note that cardiac response rates were 2–3 times higher with D-VCd, with important prognostic implications for advanced cardiac stage. Daratumumab subcutaneous formulation has been available under NHI special approval in Taiwan for NDMM; its application to AL amyloidosis requires separate approval review.

For SCD, while SCD is uncommon in Taiwan (predominantly affecting Southeast Asian immigrant populations and expatriate residents), the APOLLO protocol HSCT data from India[^3] demonstrate that post-transplant cyclophosphamide-based GVHD prophylaxis achieves excellent outcomes in resource-realistic settings; these data are relevant to transplant programs at CGMH and NTUH managing the small but growing paediatric SCD population. Age <10 years at transplant was the only significant predictor of superior outcomes (OS 100% vs 89.28%, p = 0.007), supporting early transplant referral.

Regarding thalassemia — with alpha-thalassemia carrier rates of approximately 4–5% and beta-thalassemia carriers at 1–2% in the Taiwanese population — the Diamond-Blackfan Anemia chelation data[^14] may have indirect applicability to transfusion-dependent thalassemia major patients <3 years of age who are not yet eligible for deferasirox under current NHI age thresholds (currently approved from age 2 in Taiwan for deferasirox). The finding of no myocardial iron overload despite severe hepatic iron in early-chelated patients provides reassurance that deferasirox initiated by 18 months of age in heavily transfused infants achieves cardiac protection.

The DOAC reversal audit data from the UK[^4] mirror challenges encountered at NCKUH's emergency hematology consultation service: off-label 4F-PCC use for direct factor Xa inhibitor reversal remains common in Taiwan due to limited formulary access to andexanet alfa, which is not yet covered by NHI. The 3% thromboembolism rate following 4F-PCC reversal in this series warrants pharmacovigilance documentation in institutional protocols.

Hemophilia A care in Taiwan increasingly incorporates emicizumab prophylaxis (NHI-covered for inhibitor patients since 2021, non-inhibitor extended-halflife allocation since 2023). The SIPPET 10-year legacy review[^5] raises the question of whether declining inhibitor incidence in the emicizumab era truly reflects immunological protection or deferred antigen exposure — a distinction that will become clinically critical if and when gene therapy (fitusiran, valoctocogene roxaparvovec) receives NHI consideration in Taiwan.

## XIV. Key Takeaways

The ANDROMEDA final analysis establishes the first randomized OS benefit of any therapy in newly diagnosed AL amyloidosis (HR 0.62; p = 0.0121), cementing D-VCd as a category-defining standard of care. The JMJD1C epigenetic mechanism study reframes HIT as a condition with a tractable epigenetic substrate, potentially enabling future B-cell-targeted risk stratification beyond the 4Ts score. The SIPPET 10-year legacy and miR-128 data together advance a multi-layered epigenetic framework for FVIII inhibitor formation, with direct implications for product selection in PUPs. The APOLLO HSCT series provides compelling real-world evidence that modern GVHD prophylaxis platforms achieve near-curative outcomes in paediatric SCD when transplanted before age 10. Finally, the VWD trial landscape review exposes a structural gap in female-specific endpoint capture that must be addressed by investigators and regulatory bodies to ensure equitable evidence generation for VWD management.
